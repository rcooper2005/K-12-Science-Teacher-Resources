AP Environmental Science is interdisciplinary, covering biology, chemistry, and earth science standards plus advanced college-level content.

Course Overview: AP Environmental Science (APES) is an interdisciplinary course embracing biology, geology, chemistry, and geography. Students explore the interrelationships of the natural world, identify and analyze environmental problems, evaluate risks, and propose solutions.

üéØ Big Ideas

Big Idea 1: Energy Transfer (ENG)

Energy conversions underlie all ecological processes. Energy cannot be created or destroyed but changes form as it flows through systems.

Big Idea 2: Interactions Between Earth Systems (ERT)

The Earth is one interconnected system. Natural and human-induced changes affect biogeochemical cycles.

Big Idea 3: Interactions Between Different Species and the Environment (ING)

Organisms interact with each other and their environments in complex ways. These interactions determine ecosystem structure and function.

Big Idea 4: Sustainability (SUS)

Human survival depends on developing practices that achieve sustainable systems. The goal is to meet present needs without compromising future generations.

Carbon Cycle

Carbon moves through the atmosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and lithosphere. Key processes include photosynthesis (carbon fixation), cellular respiration, decomposition, combustion, and ocean absorption. Fossil fuel combustion has increased atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420 ppm today.

Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen (N‚ÇÇ) comprises 78% of atmosphere but is unusable by most organisms. Key processes: nitrogen fixation (by bacteria and lightning), nitrification (NH‚ÇÉ ‚Üí NO‚ÇÇ‚Åª ‚Üí NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª), assimilation, ammonification, and denitrification. The Haber-Bosch process artificially fixes nitrogen for fertilizers.

Phosphorus Cycle

Unlike other cycles, phosphorus has no atmospheric component. It cycles through rock weathering, soil absorption, plant uptake, and eventual return to sediments. Phosphorus is often a limiting nutrient. Agricultural runoff causes eutrophication.

Hydrologic Cycle

Water cycles through evaporation, transpiration (evapotranspiration), condensation, precipitation, infiltration, and runoff. Human impacts include dam construction, groundwater depletion, and urbanization.

Laws of Thermodynamics in Ecosystems

First Law: Energy is neither created nor destroyed, only transformed. Second Law: Every energy transformation increases entropy; useful energy decreases.

The 10% Rule

Only about 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels. This explains why food chains are typically limited to 4-5 levels.

Gross vs. Net Primary Productivity

GPP = total energy captured by photosynthesis. NPP = GPP - respiration (energy available for consumers). Different biomes have vastly different NPP values‚Äîtropical rainforests (~2,200 g/m¬≤/yr) vs. deserts (~90 g/m¬≤/yr).

Population Growth Models

Exponential Growth: N(t) = N‚ÇÄe^(rt) ‚Äî occurs with unlimited resources. J-shaped curve.

Logistic Growth: dN/dt = rN[(K-N)/K] ‚Äî incorporates carrying capacity. S-shaped curve.

r-Selected vs. K-Selected Species

r-selected: many offspring, little parental care, short lifespan. K-selected: few offspring, extensive parental care, long lifespan.

Demographic Transition Model

Stage 1: High birth/death rates. Stage 2: Death rates fall. Stage 3: Birth rates fall. Stage 4: Low birth/death rates. Stage 5: Birth rates below death rates.

Plate Tectonics

Earth's lithosphere is divided into tectonic plates moving on the asthenosphere. Plate boundaries: divergent (spreading), convergent (subduction/collision), and transform (sliding).

Soil Science

Soil horizons: O (organic), A (topsoil), E (eluviation), B (subsoil), C (parent material), R (bedrock). Soil formation takes 200-1000 years per centimeter.

Atmosphere Structure

Troposphere (weather, 0-12 km), Stratosphere (ozone layer, 12-50 km), Mesosphere (50-85 km), Thermosphere (85-600 km).

‚ùå Common Misconceptions

üåç Ecosystems & Energy

‚ùå "Energy is recycled through ecosystems like nutrients."

‚úì Energy flows one-way through ecosystems (sun ‚Üí producers ‚Üí consumers ‚Üí heat). Only about 10% transfers between levels. Nutrients, however, ARE recycled.

üå°Ô∏è Climate & Atmosphere

‚ùå "The greenhouse effect is bad and caused by humans."

‚úì The natural greenhouse effect is essential‚Äîwithout it, Earth would be ~33¬∞C colder. The problem is the ENHANCED greenhouse effect from human emissions.

‚ùå "The ozone hole causes global warming."

‚úì These are separate issues. Ozone depletion affects UV radiation. Global warming is caused by greenhouse gases trapping heat.

‚ùå "A cold winter disproves global warming."

‚úì Weather (short-term, local) differs from climate (long-term, global patterns). Climate change can cause more extreme weather events.

üíß Water & Pollution

‚ùå "Dilution is the solution to pollution."

‚úì Many pollutants persist, bioaccumulate, or cause problems even at low concentrations.

‚ùå "Eutrophication means too much oxygen."

‚úì Eutrophication involves excess nutrients leading to algal blooms. Decomposition then DEPLETES oxygen (hypoxia).

‚ôªÔ∏è Sustainability

‚ùå "Recycling solves our waste problems."

‚úì Recycling is helpful but limited. The waste hierarchy prioritizes REDUCE and REUSE over recycling.

‚ùå "Renewable energy is completely clean."

‚úì All energy sources have impacts. Solar and wind require mining for materials. Hydroelectric alters ecosystems. They're generally better than fossil fuels, but not impact-free.
