‚òÄÔ∏è Unit 1: Light & Sound

Key Concepts for Teachers

Sound and Vibrations

Sound is produced when objects vibrate. These vibrations travel through air as sound waves. When sound waves reach our ears, the eardrum vibrates, and our brain interprets these as sound. Louder sounds come from stronger vibrations; pitch is determined by vibration frequency.

Light and Seeing

We see objects because light bounces off them (reflection) or they produce their own light. Objects in complete darkness cannot be seen. Light travels in straight lines until it hits an object.

Transparent, Translucent, and Opaque

Transparent: Lets almost all light pass through (glass, clear plastic). Translucent: Lets some light through but scatters it (wax paper, frosted glass). Opaque: Blocks all light (wood, metal, cardboard) - creates shadows.

Common Student Questions

"Can we see sound?" We can't see sound waves directly, but we can see their effects - like rice bouncing on a vibrating drum!

"Why is the sky blue?" Sunlight contains all colors. Blue light bounces around more than other colors because of tiny particles in the air (scattering).

üêæ Unit 2: Animal Structures

Key Concepts for Teachers

Structure and Function

Every external body part has a specific function for survival: claws for digging/defense, wings for flying, fins for swimming. This structure-function relationship is foundational.

Biomimicry

Scientists copy nature's designs: Velcro from burrs, airplane wings from birds. This is biomimicry!

Heredity and Variation

Offspring inherit traits from parents but aren't exact copies - natural variation exists. Siblings can look different while still resembling parents.

üåô Unit 3: Sky Patterns

Key Concepts for Teachers

The Sun's Apparent Motion

The sun appears to move east to west daily due to Earth's rotation. Position changes throughout the day: low morning, highest noon, low evening.

Moon Phases

The moon reflects sunlight, doesn't produce its own light. As it orbits Earth (~29.5 days), we see different amounts of the lit side: new moon, crescent, quarter, gibbous, full moon.

Seasonal Changes in Daylight

Earth's tilted axis causes different parts to receive varying sunlight throughout the year. Summer = more daylight; winter = less daylight.

üî¨ Lab 1: Vibrations Make Sound

1-PS4-1S1P1.a‚è±Ô∏è 35 minutes

üéØ Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

  • Demonstrate that vibrating materials produce sound
  • Observe and describe vibrations using multiple senses
  • Use evidence to explain the relationship between vibrations and sound

üì¶ Materials (per group of 4)

  • 1 rubber band, 1 plastic ruler
  • 1 tuning fork (or metal spoon), 1 cup of water
  • 1 drum or container with plastic wrap stretched over top
  • Rice or sprinkles (1 tablespoon), Recording sheet

üìù Procedure

  1. Rubber Band Test: Stretch rubber band between fingers. Pluck gently. What do you see, hear, and feel?
  2. Ruler Test: Hold ruler flat on table with half hanging over edge. Push down and release. What happens?
  3. Tuning Fork Test: Strike tuning fork gently on shoe. Touch it to water surface. What happens?
  4. Drum Test: Sprinkle rice on drum. Hit drum gently, then harder. What changes?
  5. Record observations. Draw what you see!

üîç CER Framework

Question: What causes sound?

Claim (Example): Sound is made when objects vibrate (move back and forth very quickly).

Evidence (Examples):

  • When I plucked the rubber band, I saw it wiggle AND heard a twanging sound.
  • When the ruler vibrated, it made a "boing" sound. When it stopped moving, sound stopped.
  • The tuning fork made water splash because it was vibrating.
  • Rice jumped on the drum when I hit it because the drum was vibrating.

Reasoning: All tests showed that when something vibrates, it makes sound. When vibrations stopped, sound stopped. Vibrations push air, and air carries sound to our ears.

‚úÖ Assessment Checklist

  • Student identifies vibrations in each test
  • Student connects vibrations to sound production
  • Student uses observations as evidence
  • Student explains WHY vibrations make sound

üî¨ Lab 2: Light and Shadows Investigation

1-PS4-21-PS4-3‚è±Ô∏è 40 minutes

üéØ Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

  • Explain that objects are seen when light illuminates them
  • Classify materials as transparent, translucent, or opaque
  • Predict and test how different materials affect light

üì¶ Materials

  • Flashlight, cardboard box with hole, small toy
  • Test materials: clear plastic wrap, wax paper, cardboard, tissue paper, aluminum foil
  • White paper (shadow screen), recording sheet

üìù Procedure

Part A: Can We See in the Dark?

  1. Place toy inside closed cardboard box. Can you see it through hole? (No!)
  2. Shine flashlight into box. Can you see it now? (Yes!) Why?

Part B: Material Testing

  1. Set up white paper as "shadow screen" 30cm from flashlight
  2. PREDICT: Will each material let light through?
  3. TEST: Hold each material between flashlight and screen
  4. RECORD: Sort materials into three groups

üîç CER Framework

Question: Why can we see some objects but not others? What happens when light hits different materials?

Claim: We can only see objects when light shines on them. Transparent materials let light through, translucent let some through, opaque block all light.

Evidence: Couldn't see toy in dark box until flashlight shined on it. Clear plastic let all light through. Wax paper let some light but was blurry. Cardboard blocked all light, made shadow.

Reasoning: We need light to see! Transparent materials let light pass. Translucent scatter light (blurry). Opaque block light completely, creating shadows.

üî¨ Lab 3: Design a Communication Device

1-PS4-4‚è±Ô∏è 45 minutes

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Design and build a device using light OR sound to communicate
  • Test their device and describe how it sends a message
  • Explain how light or sound helps people communicate over distances

üì¶ Materials

Light Devices: Flashlights, mirrors, colored cellophane, paper tubes

Sound Devices: Paper cups, string, paper towel tubes, rubber bands, small bells

üìù Procedure

  1. Problem: Send a message across the room without talking normally!
  2. Plan: Draw your device. Label parts. Explain how it will work.
  3. Build: Use materials to build your device.
  4. Test: Send simple message (yes/no). Did partner understand?
  5. Improve: Make one change and test again.

üîç CER Framework

Question: How can we use light or sound to communicate over a distance?

Claim: We can send messages using light signals or sound through string telephones.

Evidence: String telephone worked - partner heard me across the room. Flashlight code worked - 1 flash = yes, 2 = no. String had to be tight.

Reasoning: Sound vibrations travel through string. Light can be seen from far away. Both can send messages with a code!

üî¨ Lab 4: Animal Body Parts Investigation

1-LS1-1‚è±Ô∏è 40 minutes

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Identify external body parts of different animals
  • Explain how body parts help animals survive
  • Match animal body parts to their functions

üì¶ Materials

  • Animal picture cards (8-10 animals), Body Part Function cards, Magnifying glasses, Recording sheets

üìù Procedure

  1. Choose animal card. Look closely with magnifying glass.
  2. Draw and label external body parts: eyes, ears, mouth, legs, tail, wings, claws, fins.
  3. For each part, write what it helps the animal DO.
  4. Sort animals: Which can fly? Swim? Run fast? Climb?
  5. Compare two different animals. How are their body parts different? Why?

üîç CER Framework

Question: How do animal body parts help animals survive?

Claim: Animals have special body parts that help them survive by finding food, moving, and staying safe.

Evidence: Eagle has sharp talons to catch prey. Fish has fins to swim. Rabbit has big ears to hear predators. Turtle has shell for protection.

Reasoning: Each animal has body parts that match where it lives and what it needs. Animals that swim have fins. Flying animals have wings. Body parts and what they do go together!

üî¨ Lab 5: Parents and Offspring Comparison

1-LS3-1‚è±Ô∏è 35 minutes

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Observe similarities between parent animals and their offspring
  • Identify differences between parents and offspring
  • Explain that young animals are like, but not exactly like, their parents

üìù Procedure

  1. Choose a parent/baby animal card set
  2. Look at parent carefully. What do you notice?
  3. Look at baby. What is SAME? What is DIFFERENT?
  4. Complete Venn diagram: similarities in middle, differences on sides
  5. Look at puppies from same litter. Are they all exactly the same?

üîç CER Framework

Question: Are baby animals exactly the same as their parents?

Claim: Baby animals are like their parents, but not exactly the same.

Evidence: Puppy and dog both have fur, four legs, tail (SAME). Puppy is much smaller (DIFFERENT). Puppies from same family had different colored fur.

Reasoning: Babies inherit traits from parents so they share many things. But babies aren't exact copies - they can have different sizes, colors, patterns. This is called variation.

üî¨ Lab 6: Tracking the Sun

1-ESS1-1‚è±Ô∏è 15 min √ó 3 observations

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Observe and record the sun's position at different times of day
  • Describe the pattern of the sun's apparent movement
  • Predict where the sun will be at different times

üìù Procedure

‚ö†Ô∏è SAFETY: Never look directly at the sun!

  1. Morning (9 AM): Face south. Point to where sun is. Is it high or low? Mark on tracking sheet.
  2. Midday (12 PM): Where is sun now? Higher or lower? Mark it.
  3. Afternoon (2-3 PM): Where is sun now? Mark it.
  4. Connect marks with a line. What pattern do you see?
  5. Predict: Where will sun be at 5 PM? Draw your prediction!

üîç CER Framework

Question: Does the sun stay in one place? What pattern does it follow?

Claim: The sun appears to move across the sky each day - rises in east, highest at noon, sets in west.

Evidence: Morning: sun low on left (east). Noon: sun high in middle. Afternoon: sun lower on right (west). Tracking sheet shows arc shape.

Reasoning: The sun follows a predictable pattern every day. It appears to move east to west. This happens because Earth spins! We can use this pattern to predict where sun will be.

üìÑ Article 1: What Makes Sound?

1-PS4-1

What Makes Sound?

Have you ever wondered how we hear sounds? Sounds happen when things vibrate. Vibrate means to move back and forth very fast.

When you hit a drum, the top vibrates. The vibrations push the air. The air carries the sound to your ears!

Try this: Put your hand on your throat and hum. Do you feel the vibrations? That's your voice box making sound!

All sounds come from vibrations. When vibrations stop, sound stops too.

Questions: 1) What does "vibrate" mean? 2) What happens when you hit a drum? 3) Where can you feel vibrations when you hum?

üìÑ Article 2: Light Helps Us See

1-PS4-2

Light Helps Us See

Close your eyes. What do you see? Nothing! We need light to see things.

Some things make their own light. The sun, stars, and lamps are light sources.

Most things do not make light. We see them because light bounces off them and into our eyes.

In a dark room with no light, we cannot see anything. That's why we use flashlights at night!

Questions: 1) Why do we need light to see? 2) Name two light sources. 3) How do we see things that don't glow?

üìÑ Article 3: Animal Body Parts

1-LS1-1

Amazing Animal Body Parts

Animals have special body parts that help them survive. Survive means to stay alive.

Look at a duck! Ducks have webbed feet like paddles. This helps them swim fast. Ducks also have flat bills to scoop up food.

Eagles have sharp claws called talons to catch food. Eagles also have hooked beaks to tear their food.

Every animal has body parts that match how it lives!

Questions: 1) What does "survive" mean? 2) How do webbed feet help ducks? 3) What do eagles use talons for?

üìÑ Article 4: Babies and Parents

1-LS3-1

Babies and Parents

Look at a puppy and its mother. The puppy has four legs, just like mom. It has fur, just like mom. Baby animals are a lot like their parents!

But babies are not exactly the same. A puppy might have different colored fur. Puppies are also much smaller!

Even puppies from the same family look different from each other. This is called variation.

Babies get traits from both parents. That's why they are similar, but not exactly alike!

Questions: 1) How are puppies like their parents? 2) How might a puppy be different? 3) What is variation?

üìÑ Article 5: Patterns in the Sky

1-ESS1-1

Sky Patterns

Look up at the sky! The sun and moon follow patterns. A pattern is something that repeats the same way.

Every day, the sun rises in the east. It moves across the sky and sets in the west. This happens every single day!

The moon changes shape over time. Sometimes it looks like a big circle. Sometimes like a banana! These are called phases.

Because these are patterns, we can predict what will happen. We know the sun will rise tomorrow!

Questions: 1) What is a pattern? 2) Where does the sun rise and set? 3) Why can we predict the sun will rise?

üìñ Vocabulary

Key terms with kid-friendly definitions and examples.

‚òÄÔ∏è Light & Sound Vocabulary
Vibration

Moving back and forth very fast.

Example: A guitar string vibrates when you pluck it.

Sound

What we hear when vibrations travel through air to our ears.

Example: The sound of a bell ringing.

Light

Energy that lets us see things.

Example: The sun gives us light during the day.

Light Source

Something that makes its own light.

Example: A lamp, the sun, a candle.

Illuminate

To shine light on something.

Example: The flashlight illuminates the dark room.

Transparent

Lets all light pass through. See clearly through it.

Example: Window glass is transparent.

Translucent

Lets some light through, but things look blurry.

Example: Wax paper is translucent.

Opaque

Blocks all light. Creates shadows.

Example: A wooden board is opaque.

Shadow

A dark area when an object blocks light.

Example: Your body makes a shadow on sunny days.

Communicate

To share information or send a message.

Example: We use words and signals to communicate.

üêæ Animal Structures Vocabulary
Survive

To stay alive.

Example: Animals need food and water to survive.

External

On the outside of the body.

Example: Fur and feathers are external body parts.

Function

What something does; its job.

Example: The function of wings is to help birds fly.

Structure

A body part or how something is built.

Example: A bird's beak is a structure.

Offspring

The babies or young of a plant or animal.

Example: Puppies are the offspring of dogs.

Trait

A characteristic passed from parents to offspring.

Example: Eye color is a trait.

Variation

Differences between individuals of the same type.

Example: Puppies from same mother can have different fur colors.

Biomimicry

When people copy designs from nature to solve problems.

Example: Velcro was inspired by sticky burrs.

üåô Sky Patterns Vocabulary
Pattern

Something that repeats in the same way.

Example: The sun rising every morning is a pattern.

Predict

To make a good guess about what will happen.

Example: I predict the sun will rise in the east tomorrow.

Phase

One of the different shapes the moon appears to be.

Example: A full moon is one phase.

Sunrise

When the sun appears to come up in the morning.

Example: Sunrise happens in the east.

Sunset

When the sun appears to go down in the evening.

Example: Sunset happens in the west.

Season

A time of year with certain weather (spring, summer, fall, winter).

Example: Summer has the longest days.

Daylight

The time when the sun is up and we can see.

Example: We have more daylight in summer.

Observe

To watch carefully and notice details.

Example: Scientists observe the sky to find patterns.

üí≠ Common Misconceptions

Address these common misunderstandings to build accurate scientific thinking.

‚òÄÔ∏è Light & Sound Misconceptions

‚ùå "We can see because our eyes send out light."

‚úÖ Reality: We see because light enters our eyes. Light bounces off objects into our eyes.

Address: Dark room activity - no light source = can't see even with eyes open.

‚ùå "Sound just happens - nothing has to move."

‚úÖ Reality: All sound is caused by vibrations. Something MUST vibrate to create sound.

Address: Multiple vibration investigations. Feel throat while humming!

‚ùå "Shadows are made of something dark."

‚úÖ Reality: Shadows are areas where light is blocked. They are ABSENCE of light.

Address: Shadow experiments - remove object = no shadow!

üêæ Animal Structures Misconceptions

‚ùå "Animals choose their body parts or can change them."

‚úÖ Reality: Animals are born with their body parts. They inherit them from parents.

Address: Discuss how puppies are born with same type of parts as parents.

‚ùå "Baby animals are just small copies of their parents."

‚úÖ Reality: Babies are SIMILAR but not identical. They inherit from BOTH parents and have variations.

Address: Compare puppies from same litter - different colors, patterns!

‚ùå "All birds fly, all fish swim, etc."

‚úÖ Reality: There are exceptions! Penguins and ostriches are birds that don't fly.

Address: Show examples that break the "rules" - penguin wings for swimming!

üåô Sky Patterns Misconceptions

‚ùå "The sun moves around the Earth."

‚úÖ Reality: Earth rotates (spins), making it LOOK like the sun moves. The sun stays in place!

Address: Spin student while looking at "sun" poster - sun appears to move but student is spinning!

‚ùå "The moon makes its own light like the sun."

‚úÖ Reality: The moon reflects light from the sun - like a giant mirror!

Address: Shine flashlight on ball in dark room. Ball reflects light but doesn't glow itself.

‚ùå "The moon only comes out at night."

‚úÖ Reality: The moon is often visible during the day too!

Address: Go outside during day and look for the moon - often visible!

‚ùå "Different phases show different moons."

‚úÖ Reality: There is only ONE moon. It looks different because we see different amounts of the sunlit side.

Address: Lamp and ball model - walk ball around head, lit surface visible changes but same ball!

üå± Building AI Awareness

Help young learners understand AI exists without using it directly:

  • AI Is a Tool: Explain that AI is like other tools - it helps people do things
  • People Make AI: Scientists and engineers create AI programs
  • AI Isn't Magic: Computers follow instructions - they don't think like people
  • AI Can Make Mistakes: Just like people, AI isn't always right
  • AI Can't Do Everything: AI can't feel, taste, smell, or do science experiments

üî¨ Why Hands-On Science Matters

Emphasize that real science requires doing, not just asking a computer:

  • We need to see, touch, and explore to learn about the world
  • Computers can't smell a flower or feel how heavy something is
  • Scientists do experiments - they don't just ask computers
  • We learn best when we try things ourselves
  • AI can tell us about animals, but we need to observe them to really understand
