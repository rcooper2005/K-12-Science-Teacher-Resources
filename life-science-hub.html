S7L2: Cell Structure & Function

S7L2. Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information to describe how cell structures, cells, tissues, organs, and organ systems interact to maintain the basic needs of organisms.

  • S7L2.a. Develop a model and construct an explanation of how cell structures (nucleus, cytoplasm, cell membrane, cell wall, chloroplasts, lysosome, and mitochondria) contribute to the function of the cell.
  • S7L2.b. Develop a conceptual model of how cells are organized into tissues, tissues into organs, organs into systems, and systems into organisms.
  • S7L2.c. Construct an argument about the relationship between structures and functions of the circulatory, musculoskeletal, nervous, and digestive systems.

  • S7L3.a. Construct an explanation of how genetic information is transferred from parent to offspring in sexually reproducing organisms.
  • S7L3.b. Develop a model describing how asexual reproduction results in identical genetic information while sexual reproduction results in genetic variation.
  • S7L3.c. Use mathematical models (Punnett squares) to predict and explain patterns of inheritance.

  • S7L4.c. Analyze and interpret data to evaluate claims about the theory of evolution.

  • S7L5.b. Develop a model describing cycling of matter and flow of energy among biotic and abiotic parts of an ecosystem.
  • S7L5.c. Analyze data to predict effects of ecosystem changes on populations.

  • S7L1.a. Develop and defend a model that categorizes organisms based on common characteristics.

The Three Tenets of Cell Theory

  1. All living things are made of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function.
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

Historical Development

  • Robert Hooke (1665): First observed cells in cork
  • Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1674): First observed living cells
  • Matthias Schleiden (1838): All plants are made of cells
  • Theodor Schwann (1839): All animals are made of cells
  • Rudolf Virchow (1855): All cells come from pre-existing cells

Fundamentals of Heredity

Key Vocabulary

  • Gene: Segment of DNA coding for a specific trait
  • Allele: Different forms of a gene (B for brown, b for blue)
  • Genotype: Genetic makeup (BB, Bb, bb)
  • Phenotype: Physical expression (brown eyes)
  • Dominant: Allele that masks another (capital letter)
  • Recessive: Allele masked by dominant (lowercase)
  • Homozygous: Two identical alleles (BB or bb)
  • Heterozygous: Two different alleles (Bb)

Mendel's Laws

  • Law of Segregation: Allele pairs separate during gamete formation
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Genes inherited independently
  • Law of Dominance: One allele may mask another

The Blueprint of Life

DNA = Deoxyribonucleic Acid

  • Double helix shape (twisted ladder)
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone (sides of ladder)
  • Nitrogenous bases (rungs): A-T and G-C pairs
  • Located in the nucleus

Base Pairing Rules

Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T)

Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C)

Predicting Inheritance

Setting Up Punnett Squares

  1. Identify parent genotypes
  2. Separate alleles for each parent
  3. Place one parent's alleles across top
  4. Place other parent's alleles down left side
  5. Fill boxes by combining alleles
  6. Calculate genotypic and phenotypic ratios

Key Crosses

  • BB √ó bb: 100% Bb (all heterozygous)
  • Bb √ó Bb: 1:2:1 genotypic, 3:1 phenotypic
  • Bb √ó bb: 1:1 phenotypic (test cross)

Darwin's Theory

Four Principles of Natural Selection

  1. Variation: Individuals differ in traits
  2. Inheritance: Traits passed to offspring
  3. Overproduction: More offspring than can survive
  4. Differential Survival: Advantageous traits increase survival

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil Record: Changes over time
  • Comparative Anatomy: Homologous structures
  • Embryology: Similar development
  • Molecular Biology: DNA similarities
  • Biogeography: Species distribution

Energy in Ecosystems

Levels of Organization

Organism ‚Üí Population ‚Üí Community ‚Üí Ecosystem ‚Üí Biome ‚Üí Biosphere

Energy Flow

  • Energy enters as sunlight
  • 10% Rule: Only ~10% transfers to next level
  • Energy lost as heat at each level
  • Energy flows ONE WAY (not recycled)

Trophic Levels

  1. Producers: Make own food (100% energy)
  2. Primary Consumers: Herbivores (10%)
  3. Secondary Consumers: Carnivores (1%)
  4. Tertiary Consumers: Top predators (0.1%)
  5. Decomposers: Break down dead matter

üî¨ Lab 1: Microscope Cell Observation

Standard: S7L2.a | Duration: 45-50 min

üéØ Objectives

  • Properly use a compound microscope
  • Compare plant and animal cells
  • Identify major cell structures

üì¶ Materials

Compound microscopes, prepared slides (onion, cheek cells), fresh onion, toothpicks, methylene blue stain, blank slides, coverslips, water droppers, worksheets, colored pencils

üìã Procedure

  1. Review proper microscope handling
  2. Cheek Cells: Scrape cheek gently, smear on slide, add methylene blue, cover
  3. Onion Cells: Peel thin layer, place on slide, add water, cover
  4. Observe at low, medium, and high power
  5. Draw and label cells at each magnification

üìù CER Framework

Question: How are plant and animal cells similar and different?

Claim: Student statement answering the question

Evidence: Observations, drawings, measurements

Reasoning: Connect evidence to cell structure knowledge

ü•ö Lab 2: Osmosis Egg Lab

Standard: S7L2.a | Duration: 3-4 days (15 min/day)

üì¶ Materials

Raw eggs, white vinegar, corn syrup, distilled water, plastic cups, plastic wrap, measuring tape, balance/scale

üìã Procedure

  1. Day 1: Measure egg, place in vinegar 24-48 hours
  2. Day 2: Remove naked egg, measure, place in corn syrup
  3. Day 3: Remove, measure, place in distilled water
  4. Day 4: Final measurements, graph data

üìù CER Framework

Question: How does solution concentration affect osmosis?

üß¨ Lab 3: Strawberry DNA Extraction

Standard: S7L3.a | Duration: 45 min

üì¶ Materials

Fresh/frozen strawberries, extraction buffer (10mL dish soap, 90mL water, 1/4 tsp salt), cold isopropyl alcohol (91%), plastic bags, coffee filters, test tubes, wooden sticks, safety goggles, gloves

üìã Procedure

  1. Mush strawberry in bag for 2 minutes
  2. Add 10mL extraction buffer, mix gently 1 minute
  3. Filter through coffee filter into test tube
  4. Slowly add cold isopropyl alcohol down side
  5. Wait 2-3 minutes, observe white stringy DNA
  6. Spool out DNA with wooden stick

ü¶ã Lab 4: Natural Selection Simulation

Standard: S7L4.a, S7L4.b | Duration: 45 min

üì¶ Materials

Colored paper "prey" (20 each of 4 colors), colored fabric backgrounds, forceps, timers, data tables, graphing paper

üìã Procedure

  1. Scatter 20 of each colored prey on background
  2. Predators capture prey for 30 seconds
  3. Count survivors, record data
  4. Survivors "reproduce" - add same color
  5. Repeat for 4-5 generations
  6. Graph population changes

üå± Lab 5: Ecosystem in a Bottle

Standard: S7L5.b | Duration: Ongoing (setup 45 min)

üì¶ Materials

Clear 2-liter bottles (2 per group), aquarium gravel, soil, Elodea, small aquatic animals (snails), land plants (moss), pond water, tape, scissors

üìã Procedure

  1. Cut bottles for aquatic (bottom) and terrestrial (top) chambers
  2. Add gravel and water to aquatic section with plants
  3. Add soil and land plants to terrestrial section
  4. Connect chambers, seal system
  5. Place in indirect sunlight
  6. Observe weekly for 4-6 weeks

üìÑ The Amazing World Inside Your Cells

Lexile: 920L | Unit 1: Cells

Right now, trillions of tiny structures are working inside your body. These are called cells, the basic building blocks of all living things. Think of a cell like a miniature city. The nucleus is like city hall, containing DNA instructions. Mitochondria are the power plants, producing energy. The cell membrane is the security system, controlling what enters and exits.

Plant cells have chloroplasts that capture sunlight for photosynthesis - this is why plants can make their own food while animals cannot!

‚ùì Questions

  1. What is the main function of the nucleus?
  2. Why are mitochondria called the "powerhouse"?
  3. What organelle do plant cells have that animal cells lack?

üìÑ DNA: The Code of Life

Lexile: 950L | Unit 2: Genetics

Have you wondered why you have your mother's eyes? The answer is DNA. DNA has a double helix shape with four bases: A pairs with T, G pairs with C. Sections coding for specific traits are called genes. Different versions are alleles. Dominant alleles mask recessive ones.

‚ùì Questions

  1. What shape does DNA have?
  2. Explain the base-pairing rule.
  3. What's the difference between a gene and an allele?

üìÑ How Species Change Over Time

Lexile: 940L | Unit 3: Evolution

Charles Darwin observed that species on islands looked similar to nearby mainland species but with differences. This led to evolution theory. Natural selection works through variation, inheritance, overproduction, and differential survival. Evidence includes fossils, homologous structures, and DNA comparisons.

‚ùì Questions

  1. What are the four conditions for natural selection?
  2. Name three types of evidence for evolution.

üìÑ The Web of Life

Lexile: 930L | Unit 4: Ecology

Every living thing is connected in an ecosystem. Biotic factors are living; abiotic are non-living. Energy flows through trophic levels: producers ‚Üí primary consumers ‚Üí secondary consumers. The 10% rule means only 10% of energy transfers to each level. Unlike energy, matter is recycled!

‚ùì Questions

  1. What's the difference between biotic and abiotic?
  2. Explain the 10% rule.
  3. How is energy flow different from matter cycling?

üõ°Ô∏è General Lab Safety Rules

  • Always wear safety goggles when working with chemicals or specimens
  • No eating, drinking, or chewing gum in the lab
  • Report all spills, accidents, and injuries immediately
  • Know the location of safety equipment (eyewash, fire extinguisher, first aid)
  • Wash hands thoroughly before and after lab activities
  • Follow all written and verbal instructions carefully
  • Never taste or smell chemicals unless specifically instructed
  • Dispose of materials properly as directed

üî¨ Microscope Safety

  • Carry microscope with both hands (one on arm, one under base)
  • Start with lowest power objective, then increase magnification
  • Never force any microscope parts
  • Clean lenses only with lens paper
  • Never look into direct light through the microscope
  • Store with dust cover and lowest objective in place

üß¨ DNA Extraction Safety

  • Wear safety goggles and gloves when handling isopropyl alcohol
  • Work in well-ventilated area
  • Keep alcohol away from heat sources and flames
  • Do not ingest any materials
  • Wash hands thoroughly after lab

‚ùå Common Misconceptions

‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Cells are flat, like they appear in textbook diagrams."
‚úÖ REALITY: Cells are three-dimensional structures. Diagrams show cross-sections for clarity, but real cells have depth and volume. Using 3D models helps students visualize actual cell structure.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Plants only do photosynthesis, not cellular respiration."
‚úÖ REALITY: Plants perform BOTH processes! They photosynthesize during the day to make glucose, and perform cellular respiration 24/7 to convert that glucose into usable energy (ATP).
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Dominant traits are more common in a population."
‚úÖ REALITY: "Dominant" refers to how alleles interact, not their frequency. A dominant allele masks a recessive one, but recessive traits can be very common (like blue eyes) if the allele frequency is high in the population.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Evolution is 'just a theory' and therefore uncertain."
‚úÖ REALITY: In science, a "theory" is a well-tested explanation supported by extensive evidence. Evolution is as well-established as the theory of gravity. "Theory" in science differs from everyday usage meaning "guess."
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Organisms evolve because they need to or want to."
‚úÖ REALITY: Evolution happens to populations over generations through natural selection‚Äînot to individuals in their lifetime. Organisms don't choose to evolve; those with advantageous traits survive and reproduce more.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Humans evolved from chimpanzees."
‚úÖ REALITY: Humans and chimpanzees share a common ancestor that lived millions of years ago. We are evolutionary cousins, not parent and child. Both species evolved separately from that ancestor.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Energy is recycled in ecosystems like matter."
‚úÖ REALITY: Energy flows ONE WAY through ecosystems and is lost as heat at each trophic level (10% rule). Only MATTER (carbon, nitrogen, water) is recycled. Ecosystems need constant energy input from the sun.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "Decomposers are not important to ecosystems."
‚úÖ REALITY: Decomposers are essential! They break down dead organisms and return nutrients to the soil, allowing producers to grow. Without decomposers, ecosystems would be buried in dead material and nutrients would be locked away.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "DNA and genes are the same thing."
‚úÖ REALITY: DNA is the molecule that carries genetic information (the double helix). Genes are specific SECTIONS of DNA that code for particular traits. Think of DNA as a book and genes as individual chapters.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "All bacteria are harmful and cause disease."
‚úÖ REALITY: Most bacteria are harmless or even beneficial! Bacteria in your gut help digest food, bacteria decompose organic matter, and bacteria are used to make foods like yogurt and cheese.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: "The mitochondria is the only organelle that provides energy."
‚úÖ REALITY: While mitochondria are the main ATP producers through cellular respiration, chloroplasts in plants also capture and convert energy during photosynthesis. Different organelles work together for cell energy needs.

üåç Cultural Connections & Diverse Scientists

Highlighting the diverse contributions to biology from scientists around the world.

Rosalind Franklin

1920-1958 | British

Her X-ray crystallography work was crucial to discovering DNA's double helix structure. Photo 51, taken by Franklin, provided key evidence for Watson and Crick's model. Her contributions were largely unrecognized during her lifetime.

Connection: Unit 2 - DNA Structure

Ernest Everett Just

1883-1941 | African American

Pioneering cell biologist who made groundbreaking discoveries about cell membranes, fertilization, and cell division. Despite facing significant racism, he became one of the most accomplished biologists of his era, conducting research at the Marine Biological Laboratory.

Connection: Unit 1 - Cell Structure & Function

Barbara McClintock

1902-1992 | American

Discovered "jumping genes" (transposons) in corn, showing that genes can move within chromosomes. Her work was initially dismissed but later won the Nobel Prize in 1983. She proved that the genome is dynamic, not static.

Connection: Unit 2 - Genetics

Charles Drew

1904-1950 | African American

Developed techniques for storing blood plasma that revolutionized blood banks and saved countless lives during WWII. His work on blood preservation made modern transfusion medicine possible.

Connection: Unit 1 - Cells & Body Systems

Wangari Maathai

1940-2011 | Kenyan

Environmental and political activist who founded the Green Belt Movement, planting over 51 million trees in Kenya. First African woman to receive the Nobel Peace Prize (2004) for sustainable development and democracy.

Connection: Unit 4 - Ecosystems & Conservation

Mary Anning

1799-1847 | British

Self-taught fossil hunter who discovered some of the most important fossils of the Jurassic period, including the first complete ichthyosaur skeleton. Her discoveries greatly influenced scientific thinking about prehistoric life and evolution.

Connection: Unit 3 - Evolution & Fossil Evidence

George Washington Carver

1864-1943 | African American

Agricultural scientist who developed hundreds of products from peanuts, sweet potatoes, and soybeans. Promoted sustainable farming practices and crop rotation to help restore soil health.

Connection: Unit 4 - Ecology & Sustainability

Tu Youyou

1930-present | Chinese

Discovered artemisinin, a treatment for malaria that has saved millions of lives. Used traditional Chinese medicine knowledge to guide her research. Won Nobel Prize in 2015 for this breakthrough.

Connection: Unit 1 - Cells & Disease

üåç Cultural Contributions to Biology

Indigenous Knowledge

Many medicinal plants and ecological practices come from indigenous cultures worldwide. Traditional knowledge has contributed to modern medicine and conservation.

Ancient Civilizations

Early classification systems from ancient Greece (Aristotle), medicine from Egypt and China, and agricultural practices from Mesopotamia laid foundations for modern biology.

Islamic Golden Age

Scientists like Ibn al-Nafis described pulmonary circulation, and others made advances in medicine, pharmacology, and natural history during the medieval period.

Global Collaboration

Modern biology is a global effort. The Human Genome Project, conservation initiatives, and disease research involve scientists from every continent working together.
