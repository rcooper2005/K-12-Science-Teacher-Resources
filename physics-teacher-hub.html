üìã Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - Physics

Georgia Physics Standards Overview

The Georgia Standards of Excellence for Physics provide a rigorous framework for understanding the physical world through mathematical analysis and scientific inquiry. These standards prepare students for college-level physics and STEM careers.

Standard Description

SP1: Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about the relationship between distance, displacement, speed, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time.

Learning Targets

  • SP1.a: Plan and carry out an investigation of one-dimensional motion to calculate average and instantaneous speed and velocity.
  • SP1.b: Analyze position vs. time, velocity vs. time, and acceleration vs. time graphs and use them to solve problems.
  • SP1.c: Compare and contrast scalar and vector quantities.
  • SP1.d: Understand vectors and vector addition.
  • SP1.e: Analyze free-fall problems algebraically and graphically.
  • SP1.f: Distinguish between average and instantaneous velocity.
  • SP1.g: Investigate two-dimensional projectile motion.
  • SP1.h: Understand relative motion.
Key Concepts: Position, displacement, velocity, acceleration, vectors, motion graphs, projectile motion, free fall

Standard Description

SP2: Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about how forces affect the motion of objects.

Learning Targets

  • SP2.a: Construct free-body diagrams to identify forces on an object.
  • SP2.b: Model object interactions based on Newton's third law.
  • SP2.c: Analyze motion using Newton's second law.
  • SP2.d: Calculate static and kinetic frictional forces.
  • SP2.e: Analyze mass and gravitational force relationships.
  • SP2.f: Describe gravitational attraction using Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation.
  • SP2.g: Analyze motion on an incline.
  • SP2.h: Apply Newton's laws to circular motion.
Key Concepts: Force, Newton's Laws, free-body diagrams, friction, normal force, weight, inclined planes, circular motion, universal gravitation

Standard Description

SP3: Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about the importance of conservation laws for mechanical energy and linear momentum.

Learning Targets

  • SP3.a: Analyze the relationship between work and mechanical energy change.
  • SP3.b: Explain mechanical energy conservation when no friction is present.
  • SP3.c: Calculate work by a constant force and relate to kinetic energy change.
  • SP3.d: Model kinetic and potential energy transformations.
  • SP3.e: Calculate power.
  • SP3.f: Understand simple machines.
Key Concepts: Work, kinetic energy, potential energy, mechanical energy, conservation of energy, power, simple machines

Standard Description

SP4: Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about momentum conservation.

Learning Targets

  • SP4.a: Demonstrate conservation of momentum in collisions.
  • SP4.b: Calculate momentum and impulse.
  • SP4.c: Explain the relationship between impulse and momentum change.
  • SP4.d: Calculate momentum before/after collisions and classify as elastic/inelastic.
  • SP4.e: Analyze two-dimensional collisions.
Key Concepts: Momentum, impulse, impulse-momentum theorem, conservation of momentum, elastic/inelastic collisions

Standard Description

SP5: Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about the nature and properties of waves.

Learning Targets

  • SP5.a: Explain mechanical waves as disturbance propagation.
  • SP5.b: Relate frequency, wavelength, and speed.
  • SP5.c: Investigate reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference.
  • SP5.d: Calculate sound wave properties.
  • SP5.e: Understand the Doppler effect.
  • SP5.f: Explain standing waves, resonance, beats.
  • SP5.g: Describe the electromagnetic spectrum.
  • SP5.h: Analyze reflection and refraction of light.
Key Concepts: Wave properties, transverse/longitudinal waves, reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference, standing waves, Doppler effect, EM spectrum

SP6: Electrostatics

  • SP6.a: Analyze interactions between charged objects.
  • SP6.b: Model electric charge attraction and repulsion.
  • SP6.c: Calculate electric force using Coulomb's Law.
  • SP6.d: Describe and calculate electric fields.
  • SP6.e: Explain electric potential energy and potential.

SP7: Circuits & Magnetism

  • SP7.a: Explain current, voltage, resistance using Ohm's Law.
  • SP7.b: Calculate values in series and parallel circuits.
  • SP7.c: Calculate electric power and energy.
  • SP7.d: Explain magnetic fields from currents and magnets.
  • SP7.e: Understand electromagnetic induction.
Key Concepts: Electric charge, Coulomb's Law, electric fields, current, voltage, resistance, Ohm's Law, circuits, magnetic fields, induction

üöó The Physics of Car Safety

Lexile: 1140 | Topics: Newton's Laws, Momentum, Impulse

Car crashes demonstrate Newton's first law dramatically‚Äîpassengers continue forward when the car stops. Seatbelts provide the external force to decelerate passengers safely. The key concept is impulse: J = FŒît = Œîp. For a given momentum change, increasing time reduces force. Modern seatbelts stretch slightly, airbags deploy, and crumple zones crush‚Äîall extending collision time to reduce peak forces on passengers.

Questions: 1) Why do passengers keep moving forward in a crash? 2) How does increasing collision time reduce force? 3) Calculate impulse for a 70kg person decelerating from 25 m/s to 0.

üé¢ The Science Behind Roller Coasters

Lexile: 1120 | Topics: Energy Conservation, PE and KE

Roller coasters demonstrate energy conservation. The chain lift converts electrical energy to gravitational PE (mgh). As the train descends, PE transforms to KE (¬Ωmv¬≤). The first hill is always highest because the coaster needs enough initial PE for the entire ride. At the bottom, speed is maximum; at the top, PE is maximum. KE‚ÇÅ + PE‚ÇÅ = KE‚ÇÇ + PE‚ÇÇ explains every twist and loop.

Questions: 1) Why is the first hill always highest? 2) Where is KE maximum? PE maximum? 3) A 500kg coaster at 40m height: calculate PE and speed at bottom.

üîä How Musical Instruments Create Sound

Lexile: 1160 | Topics: Waves, Standing Waves, Resonance

All sound begins with vibration. Guitar strings create standing waves with nodes and antinodes. String frequency depends on length, tension, and mass per length: f = (1/2L)‚àö(T/Œº). Pressing a fret shortens the string (higher pitch); tightening increases tension (higher pitch). Wind instruments use vibrating air columns. The unique mix of harmonics gives each instrument its characteristic timbre.

Questions: 1) What determines pitch in a vibrating string? 2) A guitar string has f‚ÇÅ = 330Hz. What is f‚ÇÉ? 3) Why do different instruments playing the same note sound different?

‚ö° Understanding Lightning

Lexile: 1150 | Topics: Electrostatics, Electric Fields

Lightning demonstrates electrostatics. In thunderclouds, collisions separate charge‚Äîpositive rises, negative sinks. This creates huge electric potential differences (hundreds of millions of volts). The negative cloud base induces positive charge on the ground through electrostatic induction. When the electric field exceeds ~3 MV/m, air ionizes, creating the stepped leader. A return stroke surges upward at 1/3 light speed, creating the flash.

Questions: 1) How does charge separate in clouds? 2) What is electrostatic induction? 3) If flash-to-thunder time is 9 seconds, how far away was the strike?

üì° How Does GPS Know Where You Are?

Lexile: 1130 | Topics: Waves, Wave Speed, d = vt

GPS uses the simple equation d = vt. Satellites broadcast signals traveling at c = 3√ó10‚Å∏ m/s. Your receiver calculates distance from signal travel time. One satellite puts you on a sphere; two narrow it to a circle; three give two points; four fix timing errors. Since light travels 30 cm/nanosecond, timing must be precise to nanoseconds‚Äîhence atomic clocks on satellites.

Questions: 1) What basic equation is central to GPS? 2) Why are multiple satellites needed? 3) A signal takes 0.068s to arrive‚Äîhow far is the satellite?

üèÄ The Physics of the Perfect Free Throw

Lexile: 1100 | Topics: Projectile Motion, Kinematics

Free throws follow parabolic trajectories. Success requires entry angles of 38-45¬∞ from horizontal. At steeper angles, the target appears larger‚Äîoptimal release angle is ~52¬∞. Studies show elite shooters vary release angle by only ¬±2¬∞ and speed by <3%. Higher arcs are more forgiving; that's why Stephen Curry's high-arcing shots are so effective.

Questions: 1) What determines basketball trajectory? 2) Why is higher arc advantageous? 3) For v‚ÇÄ = 7.5 m/s at 52¬∞, find horizontal and vertical velocity components.

‚ùå Common Physics Misconceptions

Research shows these misconceptions are deeply held and resistant to change. Address them directly through demonstration, discussion, and conceptual conflict.

‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Heavier objects fall faster than lighter objects
‚úÖ CORRECT: In the absence of air resistance, all objects fall at the same rate (g = 9.8 m/s¬≤). Galileo demonstrated this at the Tower of Pisa. Mass does not affect free-fall acceleration.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Force is required to keep an object moving
‚úÖ CORRECT: Newton's First Law states that objects in motion stay in motion at constant velocity unless acted upon by a net force. Force is needed to change motion, not maintain it.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Current gets "used up" in a circuit
‚úÖ CORRECT: Current is the same throughout a series circuit. Charge is conserved‚Äîelectrons are not consumed. Energy is transferred to components, but current remains constant.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Cold flows into warm objects
‚úÖ CORRECT: Heat (thermal energy) flows from hot to cold objects. Cold is not a substance that flows‚Äîit's simply the absence of heat energy.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Centrifugal force is a real force
‚úÖ CORRECT: Centrifugal force is a fictitious force that appears in rotating reference frames. The real force is centripetal force, directed toward the center of the circle.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Action-reaction forces cancel out
‚úÖ CORRECT: Action-reaction pairs act on DIFFERENT objects, so they cannot cancel. Forces only cancel when they act on the same object.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: In space, there is no gravity
‚úÖ CORRECT: Gravity exists everywhere. Astronauts float because they're in free fall‚Äîthey and their spacecraft are falling around Earth together. Gravity keeps them in orbit!
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Velocity and acceleration are always in the same direction
‚úÖ CORRECT: When an object slows down, velocity and acceleration are in opposite directions. Example: A ball thrown upward has upward velocity but downward acceleration.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: The bigger object exerts more force in a collision
‚úÖ CORRECT: Newton's Third Law: Forces are always equal and opposite. A truck hitting a car exerts the same force as the car exerts on the truck. The car accelerates more because F = ma and it has less mass.
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Light waves need a medium to travel
‚úÖ CORRECT: Light is an electromagnetic wave that can travel through vacuum. Sound needs a medium, but light does not. That's how sunlight reaches Earth through space!
‚ùå MISCONCEPTION: Electricity flows at the speed of light
‚úÖ CORRECT: The electric field propagates near light speed, but electrons drift very slowly (mm/s). It's like a pipe filled with marbles‚Äîpush one in, and one comes out the other end instantly, but individual marbles move slowly.
