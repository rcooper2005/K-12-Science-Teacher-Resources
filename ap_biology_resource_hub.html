AP Biology covers all Georgia high school biology standards plus advanced college-level content.

The AP Biology curriculum is organized around four Big Ideas that provide a conceptual framework for the course.

Big Ideas

Big Idea 1: Evolution

The process of evolution drives the diversity and unity of life.

Evolution is a unifying theme in biology. Natural selection explains how populations change over time.

Key Concepts:

  • Natural selection acts on phenotypic variations in populations
  • Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium describes conditions for no evolution
  • Speciation occurs through reproductive isolation
  • Phylogenetic trees represent evolutionary relationships
Big Idea 2: Energetics

Biological systems use energy and molecular building blocks to grow, reproduce, and maintain homeostasis.

Key Concepts:

  • Enzymes catalyze reactions by lowering activation energy
  • ATP is the universal energy currency of cells
  • Photosynthesis converts light energy to chemical energy
  • Cellular respiration extracts energy from organic molecules
Big Idea 3: Information Storage & Transmission

Living systems store, retrieve, transmit, and respond to information essential to life processes.

Key Concepts:

  • DNA replication ensures accurate transmission of genetic information
  • Transcription and translation convert genetic code to proteins
  • Gene regulation occurs at multiple levels
  • Mutations provide the raw material for evolution
Big Idea 4: Systems Interactions

Biological systems interact, and these interactions possess complex properties.

Key Concepts:

  • Signal transduction pathways coordinate cellular responses
  • Feedback mechanisms maintain homeostasis
  • Population dynamics influenced by biotic and abiotic factors
  • Energy and matter flow through ecosystems

üìñ Teacher Background Knowledge

Deep content knowledge organized by major topic areas.

1. Biochemistry & Macromolecules

Essential Understanding

Living systems are composed of four major classes of macromolecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

Carbohydrates

Serve as energy sources and structural components. Monosaccharides are building blocks. Polysaccharides include starch, glycogen, cellulose, and chitin.

Lipids

Hydrophobic molecules including fats, phospholipids, and steroids. Phospholipids form cell membranes with hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails.

Proteins

Polymers of amino acids. Structure levels: Primary (sequence), Secondary (helices, sheets), Tertiary (3D folding), Quaternary (multiple chains).

Nucleic Acids

DNA and RNA are polymers of nucleotides. DNA: deoxyribose, A-T-G-C. RNA: ribose, A-U-G-C.

2. Cell Structure & Membrane Transport

Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic Cells

Prokaryotes lack membrane-bound organelles. Eukaryotes have nucleus and organelles. Endosymbiotic theory explains origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts.

Membrane Structure

Fluid mosaic model: phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins. Integral and peripheral proteins serve various functions.

Transport Mechanisms

  • Passive: Simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, osmosis
  • Active: Primary (ATP), Secondary (coupled to gradient)
  • Bulk: Endocytosis, exocytosis
Water Potential: Œ® = Œ®s + Œ®p
3. Enzymes & Metabolism

Enzyme Function

Biological catalysts that lower activation energy. Active site binds substrate. Induced fit model.

Factors Affecting Activity

  • Temperature: increases rate until denaturation
  • pH: optimal range varies by enzyme
  • Substrate concentration: rate increases until Vmax

Regulation

  • Competitive inhibition: blocks active site
  • Noncompetitive inhibition: binds allosteric site
  • Feedback inhibition: end product inhibits earlier enzyme

6. DNA, Gene Expression & Regulation

DNA Replication

Semi-conservative. Helicase unwinds, primase adds primers, DNA polymerase adds nucleotides 5' to 3', ligase joins fragments.

Transcription & Translation

Transcription: DNA ‚Üí mRNA. Translation: mRNA ‚Üí Protein via ribosomes and tRNA.

Gene Regulation

  • Prokaryotes: Operons (lac, trp)
  • Eukaryotes: Chromatin modification, transcription factors, alternative splicing, mRNA stability
7. Cell Cycle & Division

Cell Cycle

G1 ‚Üí S (DNA replication) ‚Üí G2 ‚Üí M (mitosis) ‚Üí Cytokinesis. Regulated by cyclins/CDKs and checkpoints.

Mitosis

Prophase ‚Üí Metaphase ‚Üí Anaphase ‚Üí Telophase. Produces 2 identical diploid cells.

Meiosis

Meiosis I: homologs separate. Meiosis II: sister chromatids separate. Produces 4 haploid cells. Crossing over and independent assortment create variation.

8. Genetics & Heredity

Mendelian Genetics

Law of Segregation, Law of Independent Assortment. Dominant/Recessive relationships.

Non-Mendelian Inheritance

  • Incomplete dominance, codominance
  • Multiple alleles, polygenic traits
  • Epistasis, pleiotropy
  • Sex-linked traits

9. Evolution & Natural Selection

Evidence for Evolution

Fossil record, comparative anatomy, molecular biology, biogeography, direct observation.

Mechanisms

  • Natural selection: differential survival/reproduction
  • Genetic drift: random changes (bottleneck, founder effect)
  • Gene flow: movement between populations
  • Mutation: source of new variation
Hardy-Weinberg: p¬≤ + 2pq + q¬≤ = 1; p + q = 1

Types of Selection

Directional, stabilizing, disruptive, sexual selection.

10. Ecology & Ecosystems

Population Ecology

Exponential: dN/dt = rN | Logistic: dN/dt = rN(K-N)/K

Community Interactions

Competition (-/-), Predation (+/-), Mutualism (+/+), Commensalism (+/0), Parasitism (+/-)

Energy Flow

10% rule: Only ~10% transfers between trophic levels. Energy pyramids always upright.

Biogeochemical Cycles

Carbon, nitrogen, water, phosphorus cycles. Matter cycles; energy flows.

üì∞ Reading Passages

Informational texts at Lexile 1200-1300L.

Article 1: CRISPR Gene Editing

Unit 6 Lexile 1250L

In 2012, scientists discovered how to reprogram CRISPR-Cas9 into a precise gene-editing tool. CRISPR is a natural bacterial immune system. The Cas9 protein acts as molecular scissors, cutting DNA at specific locations. Researchers can design guide RNA to direct Cas9 to any gene. Medical applications include treatments for sickle cell disease, blindness, and cancer. The technology also raises ethical concerns about germline editing.

Discussion:
  1. How does CRISPR's natural function relate to its use as a tool?
  2. What are benefits and risks of editing human genes?

Article 2: Climate Change & Ecosystems

Unit 8 Lexile 1280L

As temperatures rise, ecosystems experience dramatic shifts. Phenological mismatch occurs when species fall out of sync‚Äîoak trees leaf earlier, but great tits haven't adjusted breeding timing. Species ranges are shifting toward poles at ~17 km/decade. Coral bleaching occurs when temperatures exceed normal by just 1-2¬∞C, causing corals to expel symbiotic zooxanthellae. Climate change affects predator-prey relationships across food webs.

Discussion:
  1. How does the great tit example illustrate coevolution's vulnerability?
  2. How does coral-zooxanthellae demonstrate mutualism?

Article 3: Cancer Biology

Unit 4 Lexile 1220L

Cancer represents breakdown in cell division regulation. Cyclins and CDKs drive the cycle; tumor suppressors like p53 halt division when problems occur. Cancer requires multiple mutations‚Äîexplaining why risk increases with age. The tumor microenvironment includes angiogenesis and immune evasion. Immunotherapy and targeted therapies exploit specific mutations.

Discussion:
  1. How do cyclins and tumor suppressors function as "accelerator" and "brake"?
  2. Why are multiple mutations typically required?

Article 4: Antibiotic Resistance

Unit 7 Lexile 1240L

Antibiotic resistance demonstrates evolution in real-time. Bacteria reproduce rapidly with significant genetic variation. Mutations conferring resistance arise randomly; antibiotics select for resistant individuals. Resistance mechanisms include degrading enzymes, modified targets, and efflux pumps. Horizontal gene transfer spreads resistance between species. Agricultural antibiotic use has accelerated resistance evolution.

Discussion:
  1. Using Hardy-Weinberg, explain why resistance alleles increase during treatment.
  2. Why is this an example of directional selection?

Question:

a) Describe TWO structural features of chloroplasts essential for photosynthesis. (3 pts)

FRQ 2: Gene Expression (8 pts)

Question:

a) Describe lac operon structure and function. What happens with both glucose and lactose present? (3 pts)

b) Explain TWO differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic gene regulation. (2 pts)

Part a: Structure (promoter, operator, genes); repressor blocks without lactose; both present = low transcription (no CAP activation)

Traditional Ecological Knowledge

Indigenous communities worldwide have developed sophisticated understanding of ecological relationships through generations of observation.

Examples:

  • Pacific Northwest peoples: Detailed knowledge of salmon life cycles before Western science
  • Aboriginal Australians: Fire management practices maintained ecosystem health for tens of thousands of years
  • Amazon communities: Knowledge of medicinal plants led to pharmaceutical discoveries
  • Inuit peoples: Observations of Arctic wildlife provide crucial climate change data

Discussion: How can scientists collaborate with indigenous communities while respecting intellectual property and cultural heritage?

Guidelines for sophisticated AI use in AP science courses with college-level expectations for research and verification.

‚ö†Ô∏è AP Course AI Guidelines

  • AP Courses: College-level expectations for responsible, independent AI use
  • College Board Policy: Be aware that AP exam preparation should develop YOUR skills
  • Assignment-Specific Rules: Some assessments may restrict or prohibit AI use

‚úÖ Appropriate Academic Uses

  • Literature Review Starting Point: Identify key concepts, researchers, and seminal papers to investigate
  • Complex Concept Clarification: Get multiple explanations of difficult concepts
  • Experimental Design Feedback: Brainstorm variables, controls, and methodological considerations
  • Statistical Analysis Guidance: Understand appropriate tests and interpretation methods
  • AP Exam Preparation: Generate practice questions and identify knowledge gaps
  • Writing Enhancement: Improve clarity and structure of scientific writing
  • Cross-Disciplinary Connections: Explore how concepts connect across scientific fields
  • Research Question Development: Refine testable hypotheses and research questions

üö´ Critical Limitations of AI in Science

  • No Real Experimentation: AI cannot conduct research or collect empirical data
  • Knowledge Cutoffs: AI lacks information about recent discoveries and publications
  • Hallucinated Citations: AI frequently invents plausible-sounding but fake references
  • Methodological Oversimplification: Complex procedures may be incorrectly summarized
  • No Access to Paywalled Content: Most peer-reviewed literature isn't in training data
  • Consensus Confusion: AI may not distinguish established science from contested claims
  • Calculation Errors: AI can make mathematical mistakes with apparent confidence
  • Context Blindness: AI doesn't understand your specific experimental conditions

üî¨ Advanced AI Literacy for AP Science

  • Primary Literature Navigation: Use AI to understand papers, but always read originals
  • Citation Verification Protocol: Independently verify EVERY citation AI provides
  • Methodology Scrutiny: Critically evaluate AI-described experimental procedures
  • Statistical Rigor: Verify statistical claims and understand underlying assumptions
  • Current Research Awareness: Supplement AI with current journal alerts and preprint servers
  • Model Limitations: Understand when AI models may be unreliable in your specific domain
  • Bias Recognition: Consider what biases may exist in AI's training data for your field

üìä College-Level Verification Framework

  1. Primary Source Verification: Locate and read original peer-reviewed sources
  2. Citation Authentication: Verify every reference using Google Scholar, PubMed, or library databases
  3. Methodological Review: Assess whether described methods are scientifically sound
  4. Consensus Check: Determine if claims align with scientific consensus or represent fringe views
  5. Recency Assessment: Determine if AI's knowledge is current for fast-moving fields
  6. Expert Consultation: Cross-reference with textbooks and authoritative reviews
  7. Quantitative Validation: Independently verify calculations and statistical analyses
  8. Replication Consideration: Consider whether claims have been independently verified
