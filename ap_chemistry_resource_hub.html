AP Chemistry covers all Georgia high school chemistry standards plus advanced college-level content.

AP Chemistry is organized around six Big Ideas that represent foundational concepts.

Big Ideas Overview

Big Idea 1: Scale, Proportion, and Quantity (SPQ)

The mole allows different units to be compared. Proportional relationships inform calculations.

Enduring Understandings:

  • SPQ-1: The mole allows different units to be compared (molar mass, Avogadro's number)
  • SPQ-2: Chemical formulas identify substances by their unique combination of atoms
  • SPQ-3: Interactions between intermolecular forces influence substance properties
  • SPQ-4: Conservation of atoms allows calculations of expected amounts
  • SPQ-5: Matter is conserved in chemical and physical changes
Big Idea 2: Structure and Properties (SAP)

The structure of matter determines its properties.

Enduring Understandings:

  • SAP-1: Atoms and molecules can be identified by electron distribution and energy
  • SAP-2: The periodic table shows patterns in electronic structure
  • SAP-3: Atoms or ions bond due to interactions, forming molecules
  • SAP-4: Molecular compounds are held by covalent bonds or IMF attractions
  • SAP-5: Intermolecular forces explain physical properties
  • SAP-6: Matter exists in solid, liquid, or gas state based on particle interactions
  • SAP-7: Gas properties are explained by particle motion and forces
Big Idea 3: Transformations (TRA)

Changes in matter involve rearranging atoms following predictable patterns.

Enduring Understandings:

  • TRA-1: Substances can change into new substances through reactions
  • TRA-2: Chemical reactions can be classified by mechanisms
  • TRA-3: Some reactions may be reversed, others may not
  • TRA-4: Reaction rates depend on concentrations and properties
  • TRA-5: Many reactions proceed through multiple elementary steps
Big Idea 4-5: Energy & Thermodynamics (ENE)

Energy is conserved and can change forms. Entropy and free energy determine spontaneity.

Enduring Understandings:

  • ENE-1: Energy changes during reactions are described by ŒîH
  • ENE-2: Enthalpy changes can be calculated from bond energies and Hess's Law
  • ENE-3: Energy and entropy changes determine reaction direction
  • ENE-4: Electrical energy can be generated by or used in reactions
  • ENE-5: The relationship between ŒîG and equilibrium constant

Key Equations:

ŒîG = ŒîH - TŒîS | ŒîG¬∞ = -RT ln K
Big Idea 6: Equilibrium

Reversible reactions reach a state of dynamic equilibrium.

Key Concepts:

  • Dynamic equilibrium and equilibrium constants (K)
  • Le Ch√¢telier's Principle
  • Acid-base equilibria and buffer solutions
  • Solubility equilibria and Ksp

1. Atomic Structure & Periodicity

Quantum Numbers

Four quantum numbers describe each electron: n (principal), l (angular momentum), ml (magnetic), ms (spin). The Pauli Exclusion Principle states no two electrons can have identical sets.

Electron Configurations

Aufbau principle, Hund's rule, and Pauli exclusion govern orbital filling. Exceptions: Cr ([Ar]3d‚Åµ4s¬π) and Cu ([Ar]3d¬π‚Å∞4s¬π).

Periodic Trends

  • Atomic radius: Decreases across period, increases down group
  • Ionization energy: Increases across period, decreases down group
  • Electronegativity: Increases toward F
2. Chemical Bonding

Ionic Bonding

Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. Lattice energy: E ‚àù (Q‚ÇÅQ‚ÇÇ)/r

Covalent Bonding

Electron sharing between nonmetals. Higher bond orders mean shorter, stronger bonds.

VSEPR Theory

Electron domains repel, determining molecular geometry: linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, etc.

Hybridization

sp (linear), sp¬≤ (trigonal planar), sp¬≥ (tetrahedral), sp¬≥d (trigonal bipyramidal), sp¬≥d¬≤ (octahedral)

3. Intermolecular Forces

Types of IMFs (Increasing Strength)

  • London Dispersion: All molecules; increases with size/electrons
  • Dipole-Dipole: Polar molecules only
  • Hydrogen Bonding: H bonded to N, O, or F
  • Ion-Dipole: Between ions and polar molecules

IMF strength determines: bp, mp, vapor pressure, viscosity, surface tension, solubility.

Lab 1: Spectrophotometry & Beer's Law
Unit 12-3 Periods

Objective

Determine unknown concentration using spectrophotometry and Beer's Law.

Materials

Spectrophotometer, cuvettes, stock solution (CuSO‚ÇÑ or food dye), volumetric flasks, pipettes, unknown solution

Procedure Summary

  1. Prepare 5-6 standard solutions by serial dilution
  2. Determine wavelength of maximum absorbance
  3. Measure absorbance of each standard; create calibration curve

Lab 2: Acid-Base Titration Curves
Unit 82 Periods

Objective

Generate and analyze titration curves for strong and weak acid-base systems.

Materials

pH meter, burette, 0.10 M HCl, 0.10 M NaOH, 0.10 M acetic acid, magnetic stirrer

  1. Titrate 25 mL HCl with NaOH, recording pH every 1 mL
  2. Near equivalence, add NaOH in 0.2 mL increments
  3. Repeat with acetic acid
  4. Plot and compare both curves

üîç Common Misconceptions

Atomic Structure

‚ùå Electrons orbit like planets

‚úì Electrons exist in probability clouds (orbitals), not fixed orbits.

‚ùå Atoms are mostly solid

‚úì Atoms are mostly empty space.

Bonding

‚ùå Ionic and covalent bonds are completely different

‚úì Bonding exists on a continuum based on electronegativity difference.

‚ùå Polar bonds = polar molecule

‚úì Depends on geometry. CO‚ÇÇ has polar bonds but is nonpolar (linear).

IMFs

‚ùå Hydrogen bonds are covalent bonds

‚úì Hydrogen bonds are intermolecular forces, not bonds within molecules.

‚ùå Larger molecules always have higher boiling points

‚úì H-bonding can overcome size. H‚ÇÇO (18 g/mol, 100¬∞C) > propane (44 g/mol, -42¬∞C).

Reactions & Stoichiometry

‚ùå Coefficients indicate grams

‚úì Coefficients represent mole ratios, not mass ratios.

‚ùå Limiting reactant has smaller mass

‚úì Must calculate moles and compare to stoichiometric requirements.

Thermodynamics

‚ùå Exothermic = always spontaneous

‚úì Spontaneity depends on both ŒîH and ŒîS (ŒîG = ŒîH - TŒîS).

‚ùå Catalysts change enthalpy

‚úì Catalysts lower Ea but don't change ŒîH, ŒîG, or K.

Kinetics

‚ùå Order equals coefficients

‚úì Order must be determined experimentally. Only true for elementary steps.

‚ùå Rate-determining step is always first

‚úì It's the slowest step, which can be any step in the mechanism.

Equilibrium

‚ùå At equilibrium, concentrations are equal

‚úì Concentrations are constant but not equal. Ratio determined by K.

‚ùå At equilibrium, reaction has stopped

‚úì Equilibrium is dynamic‚Äîforward and reverse rates are equal, not zero.

Acids & Bases

‚ùå Strong acids are more concentrated

‚úì Strength and concentration are independent. Can have dilute strong acid.

‚ùå pH 7 is always neutral

‚úì pH 7 is neutral at 25¬∞C only. At body temp (37¬∞C), neutral pH ‚âà 6.8.

Electrochemistry

‚ùå Anode is always positive

‚úì Galvanic: anode negative. Electrolytic: anode positive. Oxidation always at anode.

‚ùå Electrons flow through salt bridge

‚úì Electrons flow through wire. Salt bridge allows ion migration.
