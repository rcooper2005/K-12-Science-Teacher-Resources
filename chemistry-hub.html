Your Complete Guide to Teaching High School Chemistry

üìã Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - Chemistry

These standards outline what students should know and be able to do by the end of 10th grade chemistry.

Unit 1

SC1. Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about the use of the modern atomic theory and periodic law to explain the characteristics of atoms and elements.

  • a. Evaluate merits and limitations of different atomic models (Dalton, Thomson, Rutherford, and Bohr).
  • b. Construct an argument to support the claim that the atom is composed of subatomic particles with characteristic properties.
  • c. Construct an explanation of the production of elements heavier than hydrogen by nuclear fusion.
  • d. Construct an explanation that relates the relative abundance of isotopes to atomic mass.
  • e. Develop models, including electron configuration, to predict an element's chemical properties.
  • f. Use the periodic table to predict relative properties based on electron patterns.
Unit 2

SC2. Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about the chemical and physical properties of matter resulting from the ability of atoms to form bonds.

  • a. Plan and carry out an investigation to gather evidence comparing physical and chemical properties.
  • b. Construct an argument applying principles of inter- and intra-molecular forces.
  • c. Develop and use models to predict formulas for stable, binary ionic compounds.
  • d. Plan and carry out an investigation to determine the empirical formula of a compound.
  • e. Develop a model to illustrate energy release or absorption from a chemical reaction.
Unit 3

SC3. Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about how the Law of Conservation of Matter is used to determine chemical composition.

  • a. Use mathematics to balance chemical reactions and construct explanations for reaction outcomes.
  • b. Plan and carry out an investigation to demonstrate evidence of the Law of Conservation of Matter.
  • c. Use mathematics to apply concepts of the mole and Avogadro's number.
Unit 4

SC4. Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about quantitative relationships in chemical formulas and reactions.

  • a. Use mathematics to solve problems involving the mole concept.
  • b. Use mathematics to identify and solve reaction stoichiometry problems.
  • c. Use mathematics to determine limiting reactant and theoretical yield.
  • d. Plan and carry out an investigation to calculate percent yield and analyze experimental error.
Unit 5

SC5. Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about Kinetic Molecular Theory to explain properties of solutions.

  • a. Develop a model to illustrate dissolution by referencing intermolecular forces.
  • b. Plan and carry out an investigation to evaluate factors affecting dissolution rate.
  • c. Use mathematics to evaluate and compare concentrations (molarity, molality, normality).
  • d. Develop and use models to explain properties of acids and bases.
  • e. Plan and carry out an investigation to explore acid-base neutralization.
Unit 5

SC6. Obtain, evaluate, and communicate information about Kinetic Molecular Theory to explain behavior of gases.

  • a. Plan and carry out an investigation to calculate pressure, volume, temperature, and moles using Ideal Gas Law.
  • b. Use mathematics to analyze gas behavior and compare masses, moles, and volumes at STP.
  • c. Develop a model to illustrate motion and behavior of gas particles.

Unit 1

Historical Development of Atomic Theory

  • Dalton (1803): Atoms are indivisible spheres; all atoms of an element are identical.
  • Thomson (1897): "Plum pudding" model - electrons embedded in positive sphere.
  • Rutherford (1911): Nuclear model with dense positive nucleus.
  • Bohr (1913): Electrons in fixed circular orbits with quantized energy levels.
  • Quantum Model: Electrons in probability clouds (orbitals).

Unit 1: Atomic Structure

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Observe characteristic flame colors produced by different metal ions
  • Relate flame color to electron energy transitions
  • Identify unknown metal ions based on flame test results

üß™ Materials

  • Bunsen burner, Nichrome wire loops
  • 1M HCl, Solutions: LiCl, NaCl, KCl, CaCl‚ÇÇ, SrCl‚ÇÇ, BaCl‚ÇÇ, CuCl‚ÇÇ
  • Unknown solution, Watch glasses, Safety goggles

üìã Procedure

  1. Clean nichrome wire by dipping in HCl and heating until no color appears
  2. Dip clean wire into salt solution
  3. Hold wire in hottest part of flame and observe color
  4. Record observations; clean wire between tests
  5. Test unknown and identify by comparing colors

üìù CER Framework

Question: How can flame tests identify unknown metal ions?

Claim: Each metal ion produces a characteristic flame color for identification because...

Evidence: Record colors: Li=red, Na=yellow, K=violet, Ca=orange, Sr=red, Ba=green, Cu=blue-green

Reasoning: Heated electrons jump to excited states; returning to ground state releases specific wavelengths of light based on electron configuration.

Unit 4: Stoichiometry

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Apply stoichiometry to predict product amounts
  • Calculate percent yield

üìã Procedure

Reaction: Fe(s) + CuSO‚ÇÑ(aq) ‚Üí FeSO‚ÇÑ(aq) + Cu(s)

  1. Measure mass of clean iron nail
  2. Prepare 50 mL of 0.5 M CuSO‚ÇÑ solution

  4. Remove, rinse, dry, and weigh nail
  5. Filter and weigh copper produced

Question: How can stoichiometry predict product amount?

Unit 5: Acids & Bases

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Perform an acid-base titration
  • Determine concentration of unknown acid
  • Identify equivalence point using indicators

üìã Procedure

  1. Fill burette with standardized 0.1 M NaOH
  2. Pipette 25.00 mL unknown HCl into flask
  3. Add 2-3 drops phenolphthalein
  4. Slowly add NaOH while swirling until pink persists
  5. Record volume; repeat 2 more times

üìù CER Framework

Question: How can titration determine unknown acid concentration?

Unit 5: Gas Laws

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Investigate P-V-T relationships
  • Verify Boyle's Law experimentally

üìã Procedure

  1. Draw 50 mL air into syringe; seal with stopper
  2. Record initial volume at 1 atm
  3. Place syringe vertically, plunger down
  4. Add textbooks one at a time; record volume
  5. Graph P vs V and P vs 1/V
  6. Calculate PV products (should be constant)

üìù CER Framework

Question: What is the relationship between pressure and volume at constant temperature?

Claim: P and V are inversely proportional (Boyle's Law)

Reasoning: Decreased volume = more frequent collisions with walls = increased pressure.

Unit 4: Stoichiometry

üìã Procedure

  1. Measure mass of clean, dry crucible and lid
  2. Add ~3g CuSO‚ÇÑ¬∑5H‚ÇÇO; measure exact mass
  3. Heat crucible with lid slightly ajar for 10 minutes
  4. Cool completely; measure mass
  5. Reheat until constant mass achieved
  6. Calculate mass of water lost and percent composition

üìù CER Framework

Question: How can heating determine hydrate formula?

Reasoning: Heating drives off water as steam; mass lost = mass of water; molar masses give mole ratio.

Unit 3: Chemical Reactions

üìã Procedure

  1. Set up two beakers with different metal/solution pairs
  2. Place metal electrodes in corresponding solutions
  3. Connect with wire and voltmeter
  4. Complete circuit with salt bridge
  5. Record voltage; test different metal combinations
  6. Rank metals by activity based on voltages

üìù CER Framework

Question: What determines galvanic cell voltage?

Claim: Voltage depends on reactivity difference between metals

Reasoning: More reactive metals lose electrons more easily; difference in tendency creates electrical potential.

Unit 1

The Quest to Build the Periodic Table

In 1869, Dmitri Mendeleev created an organizational system for elements still used today. With only 63 elements known, he noticed that when arranged by atomic mass, properties repeated in patterns called periodicity.

Mendeleev left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties. When gallium was discovered in 1875, its properties matched his predictions almost exactly. Today's table uses atomic number instead of mass, containing 118 confirmed elements.

Comprehension Questions:
  1. What pattern did Mendeleev observe?
  2. How did leaving gaps show scientific thinking?
  3. What change was made to the modern table?
Unit 2

The Hidden Forces Holding Molecules Together

Chemical bonds determine nearly every property a substance has. In ionic bonds, one atom gives electrons to another, creating charged ions. Covalent bonds form when atoms share electrons.

Beyond bonds within molecules, intermolecular forces act between separate molecules. Hydrogen bonds explain why water has such unusual properties - its high boiling point and why ice floats.

Comprehension Questions:
  1. What's the difference between ionic and covalent bonds?
  2. Why does table salt form a crystal structure?
  3. How do intermolecular forces differ from chemical bonds?
Unit 3

When Atoms Rearrange: Understanding Chemical Reactions

Scientists have identified five main types of chemical reactions. Synthesis combines substances; decomposition breaks them apart. Single replacement swaps one element; double replacement swaps parts between compounds. Combustion burns fuel in oxygen.

All reactions follow the Law of Conservation of Mass - atoms present before must all be present after, just rearranged.

Comprehension Questions:
  1. What's the difference between synthesis and decomposition?
  2. Give an example of combustion in everyday life.
  3. Why must we balance chemical equations?
Unit 4

The Mole: Chemistry's Counting Unit

One mole equals 6.022 √ó 10¬≤¬≥ particles - Avogadro's number. This connection between mass and particle count makes stoichiometry possible - the math of chemical reactions.

Real reactions rarely produce the theoretical maximum. Percent yield compares actual yield to theoretical yield to evaluate efficiency.

Comprehension Questions:
  1. Why did scientists need a new counting unit for atoms?
  2. What's the relationship between molar mass and atomic mass?
  3. Why is percent yield usually less than 100%?
Unit 5

Acids, Bases, and the pH Scale

Acids produce H‚Å∫ ions; bases produce OH‚Åª ions. The pH scale runs from 0-14, with 7 being neutral. Each number represents a tenfold change in H‚Å∫ concentration.

When acids and bases combine, they undergo neutralization, producing water and a salt.

Comprehension Questions:
  1. What ions do acids and bases produce?
  2. If pH 4, how many times more acidic than pH 6?
  3. What happens during neutralization?
Unit 5

The Behavior of Gases

The kinetic molecular theory describes gases as particles in constant, random motion. Boyle's Law states pressure and volume are inversely related. Charles's Law describes volume-temperature relationships.

The Ideal Gas Law (PV=nRT) combines all relationships. Temperature must be in Kelvin.

Comprehension Questions:
  1. According to kinetic theory, why do gases fill containers?
  2. What happens to volume if you double pressure at constant T?
  3. Why must temperature be in Kelvin?

üìñ Chemistry Vocabulary

50+ essential terms organized by unit.

üîµ Unit 1: Atomic Structure

Atom - Smallest unit of an element that retains its properties
Proton - Positive subatomic particle in nucleus
Neutron - Neutral subatomic particle in nucleus
Electron - Negative particle in electron cloud
Atomic Number - Number of protons; defines element
Mass Number - Protons + neutrons
Isotope - Atoms of same element with different neutrons
Electron Configuration - Arrangement of electrons in orbitals
Valence Electrons - Outermost electrons; determine bonding
Atomic Radius - Half the distance between two bonded nuclei
Ionization Energy - Energy to remove an electron
Electronegativity - Attraction for bonding electrons

üü£ Unit 2: Chemical Bonding

Ionic Bond - Electron transfer between metal and nonmetal
Covalent Bond - Electron sharing between nonmetals
Metallic Bond - Sea of delocalized electrons
Lewis Structure - Diagram showing valence electrons and bonds
Octet Rule - Atoms tend to have 8 valence electrons
Polyatomic Ion - Group of bonded atoms with charge
Intermolecular Forces - Attractions between molecules
Hydrogen Bond - Strong IMF with H bonded to N, O, or F
Polar - Unequal electron distribution
Nonpolar - Equal electron distribution

‚ö†Ô∏è Laboratory Safety Guidelines

Essential safety information for chemistry labs.

‚ö†Ô∏è GENERAL LAB RULES
  • Always wear safety goggles and lab apron
  • No food or drink in lab
  • Know location of safety equipment (eyewash, shower, fire extinguisher)
  • Never work alone; teacher must be present
  • Read all procedures before beginning
  • Report all accidents immediately
  • Read labels carefully before using any chemical
  • Never taste or smell chemicals directly
  • Pour acids into water, never water into acids
  • Use fume hood for volatile chemicals
  • Dispose of chemicals properly - follow teacher instructions
  • Wash hands after handling any chemicals
  • Tie back long hair and loose clothing
  • Check connections before lighting
  • Light match first, then turn on gas
  • Adjust air intake for blue flame
  • Never leave flame unattended
  • Turn off gas when finished
  • Always add acid to water (AAW - "Do as you oughta, add acid to water")
  • Wear gloves when handling concentrated acids/bases
  • Neutralize spills before cleanup
  • Rinse skin immediately if contact occurs (15 minutes minimum)
  • Keep neutralizing agents nearby
  • Safety Goggles: ALWAYS required - protects against splashes and fumes
  • Lab Apron: Protects clothing and skin
  • Gloves: Required for corrosive chemicals, hot materials
  • Closed-toe Shoes: Required at all times in lab

‚ùå Common Misconceptions

Address these frequently held incorrect ideas.

‚ùå Misconception

"Atoms can be seen with microscopes."

‚úì Correction

Atoms are too small for light microscopes. Specialized instruments like electron microscopes or scanning probe microscopes can image atoms indirectly.

‚ùå Misconception

"Electrons orbit the nucleus like planets around the sun."

‚úì Correction

Electrons exist in probability clouds (orbitals) around the nucleus. Their exact position cannot be determined - only the probability of finding them in a region.

‚ùå Misconception

"Chemical bonds store energy."

‚úì Correction

Breaking bonds requires energy; forming bonds releases energy. The energy "stored" is the energy needed to break bonds, not energy contained within them.

‚ùå Misconception

"Mass is lost during chemical reactions."

‚úì Correction

Mass is always conserved in chemical reactions. If mass appears lost, gas has escaped or been absorbed from the surroundings.

‚ùå Misconception

"All acids are dangerous."

‚úì Correction

Many acids are safe at low concentrations. Citric acid (lemons), acetic acid (vinegar), and ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) are consumed daily. Concentration and type determine danger.

‚ùå Misconception

"Dissolving is a chemical change."

‚úì Correction

Dissolving is usually a physical change. The solute can be recovered by evaporating the solvent. No new substances are formed.

‚ùå Misconception

"The mole is a unit of mass."

‚úì Correction

The mole is a counting unit (6.022 √ó 10¬≤¬≥ particles). It's related to mass through molar mass but is fundamentally about quantity, not mass.

‚ùå Misconception

"In ionic compounds, electrons are shared."

‚úì Correction

In ionic compounds, electrons are transferred, not shared. One atom gains electrons (becomes negative) while another loses them (becomes positive).

‚ùå Misconception

"Gases have no mass."

‚úì Correction

All matter, including gases, has mass. Gases are less dense than solids/liquids but still have measurable mass.

‚ùå Misconception

"pH 7 is always neutral."

‚úì Correction

pH 7 is neutral only at 25¬∞C. The neutral point changes with temperature because water's ionization changes with temperature.
