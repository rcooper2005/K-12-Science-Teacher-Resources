üìö Teacher Background Knowledge

Expand each section below to review key content knowledge for each physical science unit.

Atomic Structure

All matter is composed of atoms. Atoms consist of three subatomic particles:

  • Protons: Positive charge (+1), located in the nucleus, relative mass of 1 amu. The number of protons defines the element.
  • Neutrons: No charge (neutral), located in the nucleus, relative mass of 1 amu.
  • Electrons: Negative charge (-1), located in electron clouds outside the nucleus, negligible mass.

The Periodic Table

Elements are organized by increasing atomic number into periods (rows) and groups/families (columns). Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties due to the same number of valence electrons.

Physical Changes

Physical changes alter the form or appearance but not chemical composition. Examples: melting, dissolving, cutting. Usually reversible.

Chemical Changes

Chemical changes result in new substances with different properties. Signs include: color change, gas production, precipitate formation, energy change, odor change. Usually irreversible.

Types of Chemical Reactions

  • Synthesis: A + B ‚Üí AB
  • Decomposition: AB ‚Üí A + B
  • Single Replacement: A + BC ‚Üí AC + B
  • Double Replacement: AB + CD ‚Üí AD + CB
  • Combustion: Fuel + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO

Law of Conservation of Mass

Matter cannot be created or destroyed. Total mass of reactants equals total mass of products. This is why we balance equations.

First Law (Inertia)

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by an unbalanced force. Mass is a measure of inertia.

Second Law (F = ma)

Force equals mass times acceleration. Acceleration is directly proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass.

Third Law (Action-Reaction)

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always come in pairs acting on different objects.

Key Formulas

  • Speed: s = d/t (m/s)
  • Velocity: Speed with direction (vector)
  • Acceleration: a = Œîv/t (m/s¬≤)

Motion Graphs

Position-Time: Slope = velocity. Velocity-Time: Slope = acceleration, area under curve = displacement.

Key Formulas

  • Work: W = F √ó d (Joules)
  • Kinetic Energy: KE = ¬Ωmv¬≤
  • Potential Energy: PE = mgh
  • Power: P = W/t (Watts)

Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. Total energy in a closed system remains constant.

The Six Simple Machines

  • Lever: Rigid bar pivoting around a fulcrum
  • Wheel and Axle: Wheel attached to smaller axle
  • Pulley: Rope around grooved wheel
  • Inclined Plane: Sloped surface
  • Wedge: Two inclined planes back-to-back
  • Screw: Inclined plane wrapped around cylinder

Mechanical Advantage: MA = Output Force √∑ Input Force

Efficiency: (Work Output √∑ Work Input) √ó 100%

Types of Waves

  • Transverse: Medium moves perpendicular to wave direction (light, water surface)
  • Longitudinal: Medium moves parallel to wave direction (sound)

Wave Properties

  • Wavelength (Œª): Distance between consecutive crests
  • Amplitude: Maximum displacement; related to energy
  • Frequency (f): Waves per second (Hertz)

Wave Speed: v = f √ó Œª

EM Waves (Low to High Frequency)

  1. Radio Waves: Broadcasting, communication
  2. Microwaves: Cooking, cell phones
  3. Infrared: Heat, remote controls
  4. Visible Light: ROYGBIV
  5. Ultraviolet: Sunburns, sterilization
  6. X-rays: Medical imaging
  7. Gamma Rays: Cancer treatment, nuclear

As frequency ‚Üë ‚Üí wavelength ‚Üì ‚Üí energy ‚Üë

Sound Waves

Longitudinal mechanical waves requiring a medium. Pitch = frequency; Loudness = amplitude. Speed in air: ~343 m/s

Light Behaviors

  • Reflection: Bounces off surface (angle in = angle out)
  • Refraction: Bends when entering new medium
  • Absorption: Energy converted to heat
  • Diffraction: Bends around obstacles

Unit 1 Lab 1: Density Column Investigation

üìé Objectives

  • Calculate density of various liquids
  • Predict and observe layering based on density
  • Connect density to particle arrangement

üîß Materials (per group)

  • Clear tall cylinder
  • Honey, corn syrup, dish soap (50mL each)
  • Colored water, vegetable oil, rubbing alcohol (50mL each)
  • Small objects: grape, cork, plastic bead, metal bolt
  • Balance, graduated cylinder

üìù Procedure

  1. Calculate density of each liquid (mass/volume)
  2. Predict layering order based on density
  3. Carefully pour each liquid, starting with densest
  4. Allow settling, record observations
  5. Drop small objects, observe where they settle

üìä CER Framework

Question: How does density determine liquid layering?

Claim: Liquids layer with most dense at bottom.

Evidence: Density values and observed layering order.

Reasoning: Denser liquids have more mass per volume, sinking below less dense liquids due to gravity.

Unit 2 Lab 2: Types of Chemical Reactions

üìé Objectives

  • Observe evidence of chemical reactions
  • Classify reactions by type
  • Write balanced equations

üîß Materials

  • Test tubes, baking soda, vinegar
  • Steel wool, hydrogen peroxide, yeast
  • Lead nitrate, potassium iodide solutions
  • Safety goggles, gloves

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety

  • Wear goggles and gloves always
  • Lead nitrate‚Äîteacher demo only
  • Proper chemical disposal

üìù Procedure

Station 1 (Decomposition): Add yeast to H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ‚Äîobserve gas

Station 2 (Synthesis): Heat steel wool‚Äîobserve color change

Station 3 (Double Replacement): Mix solutions‚Äîobserve precipitate

üìä CER Framework

Question: What evidence indicates a chemical reaction?

Claim: Chemical reactions produce observable changes.

Evidence: Color change, gas production, precipitate formation.

Reasoning: These indicate new substances formed as bonds broke and reformed.

Unit 2 Lab 3: Conservation of Mass

üîß Materials

  • Sealable plastic bags
  • Baking soda, vinegar
  • Digital balance

üìù Procedure

  1. Place baking soda in container inside sealed bag
  2. Add vinegar, seal completely
  3. Record mass before mixing
  4. Mix reactants, observe reaction
  5. Record mass after reaction
  6. Compare to open container results

üìä CER Framework

Claim: Mass is conserved in closed systems.

Evidence: Mass before and after reaction were equal in sealed bag.

Reasoning: Atoms rearrange but aren't created or destroyed. In open systems, gases escape‚Äîappearing as mass loss.

Unit 3 Lab 4: Egg Drop - Newton's Laws

üìé Objectives

  • Apply Newton's Laws to protect an egg
  • Design device that maximizes impulse time
  • Analyze forces during collision

üîß Materials

  • Raw eggs, cardboard, newspaper
  • Bubble wrap, foam, cotton balls
  • Tape, rubber bands, string, straws

üìù Procedure

  1. Research Newton's Laws and impulse
  2. Design protective container (20cm max)
  3. Draw labeled diagram explaining physics
  4. Build prototype, test from 1m, 2m, 3m
  5. Analyze and redesign if needed

üìä CER Framework

Claim: Designs that increase impact time and distribute force protect better.

Reasoning: F=ma shows force depends on acceleration. Increasing deceleration time reduces force.

Unit 3 Lab 5: Newton's Second Law (F=ma)

üîß Materials

  • Dynamics cart, track
  • String, pulley, mass sets
  • Stopwatch, meter stick

üìù Procedure

Part A: Keep cart mass constant, vary hanging mass (force), measure acceleration

Part B: Keep force constant, add mass to cart, measure acceleration

Graph Force vs. Acceleration and Mass vs. Acceleration

üìä CER Framework

Claim: F=ma; acceleration is directly proportional to force, inversely to mass.

Evidence: Graph analysis showing linear F-a relationship, inverse m-a relationship.

Unit 4 Lab 6: Simple Machines Efficiency

üîß Materials

  • Inclined plane, pulley systems
  • Spring scales, mass sets
  • Lever and fulcrum, meter stick

üìù Procedure

Station 1 - Inclined Plane: Measure effort force up ramp, calculate MA and efficiency

Station 2 - Pulley: Test different configurations, measure forces and distances

Station 3 - Lever: Vary fulcrum position, measure effort force

üìä CER Framework

Claim: Simple machines reduce effort force but not total work; efficiency < 100% due to friction.

Unit 5 Lab 7: Wave Properties with Slinky

üîß Materials

  • Metal and plastic slinkies
  • Meter stick, stopwatch
  • Masking tape, open floor space

üìù Procedure

Part A: Create transverse waves (side-to-side) and longitudinal waves (push-pull)

Part B: Generate waves with different amplitudes and frequencies; measure wavelength and speed

Part C: Compare wave speed in stretched vs. loose slinky, metal vs. plastic

üìä CER Framework

Claim: Frequency and wavelength are inversely related at constant speed. Wave speed depends on medium.

Unit 5 Lab 8: Light Reflection & Refraction

üîß Materials

  • Plane mirror, laser pointer
  • Protractor, white paper
  • Glass/acrylic block, water

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety

  • Never look directly into laser
  • Handle glass carefully

üìù Procedure

Part A - Reflection: Shine light at mirror at various angles, measure incident and reflected angles

Part B - Refraction: Shine light through glass block at angle, trace path, measure angles

üìä CER Framework

Claim: Angle of incidence = angle of reflection. Light bends toward normal entering denser medium.

Reasoning: Light slows in denser materials, causing bending.

The Invisible World of Atoms Lexile 950

Everything around you is made of tiny particles called atoms. About 100 million atoms could fit across your fingernail. John Dalton proposed that all matter consists of atoms in the early 1800s.

J.J. Thomson discovered electrons in 1897‚Äînegative particles within atoms. Then Ernest Rutherford's gold foil experiment proved atoms have a dense, positive nucleus. The nucleus contains protons and neutrons.

Atoms are mostly empty space! If an atom were a football stadium, the nucleus would be a marble at center field.

Questions: 1) What did Rutherford's experiment prove? 2) Compare Thomson's model to the modern model. 3) Why are atoms "mostly empty space"?

When Atoms Rearrange: Chemical Reactions Lexile 920

When you cook an egg or burn a candle, you witness chemical reactions. Atoms rearrange to form new substances. Starting materials are reactants; new substances are products.

Signs of chemical reactions: color change, gas bubbles, precipitate formation, temperature change, new smells. The Law of Conservation of Mass states atoms can't be created or destroyed‚Äîthis is why equations must be balanced.

Questions: 1) What evidence indicates a chemical reaction? 2) Explain Conservation of Mass. 3) How is synthesis different from decomposition?

Newton's Laws: The Rules of Motion Lexile 980

Newton's First Law: Objects at rest stay at rest, objects in motion stay in motion unless acted on by unbalanced force. This is inertia.

Newton's Second Law: F = ma. More force = more acceleration. More mass = less acceleration.

Newton's Third Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always come in pairs acting on different objects.

Questions: 1) Give an example of inertia. 2) If mass doubles but force is constant, what happens to acceleration? 3) Why don't action-reaction forces cancel?

Energy: The Ability to Do Work Lexile 940

Kinetic energy is energy of motion (KE = ¬Ωmv¬≤). Speed matters a lot‚Äîdouble your speed, quadruple your kinetic energy!

Potential energy is stored energy based on position (PE = mgh). The Law of Conservation of Energy states energy can't be created or destroyed, only transformed.

On a roller coaster, PE at the top transforms to KE at the bottom. That's why the first hill is always tallest!

Questions: 1) What's the difference between KE and PE? 2) Why does doubling speed quadruple KE? 3) Explain energy transformation on a coaster.

Simple Machines: Working Smarter Lexile 910

Simple machines make work easier by changing force amount, direction, or distance. The six simple machines: lever, wheel and axle, pulley, inclined plane, wedge, screw.

Mechanical advantage equals output force divided by input force. Simple machines don't reduce total work‚Äîthey make it more manageable. Real machines are never 100% efficient due to friction.

Questions: 1) How does an inclined plane make work easier? 2) What does MA > 1 mean? 3) Why can no machine be 100% efficient?

Waves: Energy in Motion Lexile 960

Waves transfer energy without transferring matter. Transverse waves have particles moving perpendicular to wave direction. Longitudinal waves have particles moving parallel.

Properties: wavelength (distance crest to crest), amplitude (max displacement = energy), frequency (waves per second in Hz).

Wave equation: v = f √ó Œª. If frequency increases and speed is constant, wavelength must decrease.

Questions: 1) How do waves transfer energy without matter? 2) What's the difference between transverse and longitudinal? 3) If frequency doubles, what happens to wavelength?

‚ö†Ô∏è Lab Safety Guidelines

General and topic-specific safety protocols for physical science labs.

üõ°Ô∏è General Lab Safety Rules

  • Always wear safety goggles when working with chemicals or projectiles
  • Tie back long hair and secure loose clothing
  • Never eat, drink, or chew gum in the lab
  • Know the location of safety equipment (eyewash, fire extinguisher, first aid)
  • Report all accidents and spills immediately
  • Follow all teacher instructions carefully
  • No horseplay or running in the lab
  • Clean up workspace and wash hands after every lab

Chemical Safety

  • Wear goggles and gloves when handling chemicals
  • Never taste or smell chemicals directly
  • Add acid to water, never water to acid
  • Dispose of chemicals properly‚Äînever down the drain unless instructed
  • Read all labels before using chemicals
  • Keep flammable materials away from heat sources

Bunsen Burner Safety

  • Clear area of flammable materials before lighting
  • Light match first, then turn on gas
  • Adjust flame to blue cone (hottest part)
  • Never leave lit burner unattended
  • Turn off gas at source when finished
  • Allow equipment to cool before touching

Electrical Safety

  • Never use damaged cords or equipment
  • Keep water away from electrical sources
  • Unplug by the plug, not the cord
  • Don't overload circuits
  • Use only equipment as intended

Motion/Projectile Lab Safety

  • Clear area before launching projectiles
  • Never aim at people or faces
  • Wear eye protection
  • Keep hands and fingers clear of moving parts
  • Secure heavy objects that could fall
  • Use appropriate footwear for motion labs

Light/Laser Safety

  • Never look directly into a laser beam or bright light source
  • Never point lasers at eyes, faces, or reflective surfaces toward people
  • Use class-appropriate lasers only (Class 2 or lower)
  • Control beam path at all times

‚ùå Common Misconceptions

Address these common student misconceptions in your teaching.

Heavier objects fall faster than lighter objects.
In the absence of air resistance, all objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass. Galileo demonstrated this, and astronauts confirmed it on the Moon. Air resistance can make lightweight objects fall slower.
Cold is transferred like heat (e.g., "cold air coming in").
Cold is not a substance that moves. Heat energy always transfers from warmer to cooler objects. What we feel as "cold" is actually heat leaving our body.
Atoms can be seen with a regular microscope.
Atoms are much too small to see with optical microscopes. They can only be "seen" with special instruments like electron microscopes or scanning tunneling microscopes, which create images indirectly.
Chemical reactions destroy matter or create new matter.
The Law of Conservation of Mass states matter cannot be created or destroyed. Atoms are only rearranged in chemical reactions. The same atoms exist before and after‚Äîjust in different arrangements.
Objects need a constant force to keep moving.
Newton's First Law: Objects in motion stay in motion unless acted on by unbalanced force. No force is needed to maintain constant velocity. Force is only needed to change motion (accelerate).
Action-reaction forces cancel each other out.
Action-reaction forces act on DIFFERENT objects, so they can't cancel. When you push a wall, you and the wall are separate objects experiencing opposite forces.
Energy is "used up" or created.
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. When we "use" energy, we convert it from one form to another (often to heat due to friction).
Simple machines reduce the total work required.
Simple machines make work easier by changing force or distance, but the total work remains the same (or more due to friction). They allow us to use less force over a greater distance.
Sound travels through space/vacuum.
Sound is a mechanical wave that requires a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel. Space is a vacuum with no particles, so sound cannot travel through it.
Light travels instantaneously.
Light travels very fast (3√ó10‚Å∏ m/s) but not instantaneously. It takes about 8 minutes for sunlight to reach Earth and over 4 years to reach the nearest star.
All radiation is dangerous.
The electromagnetic spectrum includes many types of radiation. Visible light, radio waves, and infrared are generally safe. Only high-energy radiation (UV, X-rays, gamma rays) can be harmful at high doses.
Electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed paths like planets.
The Bohr model (planetary) is simplified. Modern quantum mechanics shows electrons exist in probability clouds‚Äîwe can only predict where they're likely to be, not exact paths.

üåç Cultural Connections in Science

Diverse scientists and contributions from various cultures to physics and chemistry.

MC

Marie Curie (1867-1934)

Poland/France | Physics & Chemistry

First woman to win a Nobel Prize, and only person to win in two different sciences (Physics 1903, Chemistry 1911). Discovered polonium and radium. Pioneered research on radioactivity.

LL

Lewis Howard Latimer (1848-1928)

United States | Electrical Engineering

African American inventor who improved the carbon filament in light bulbs, making electric lighting practical for widespread use. Worked with both Edison and Bell.

CW

Chien-Shiung Wu (1912-1997)

China/United States | Nuclear Physics

Chinese American physicist who disproved the law of conservation of parity‚Äîa fundamental concept in physics. Known as the "First Lady of Physics." Worked on the Manhattan Project.

AE

Albert Einstein (1879-1955)

Germany/United States | Theoretical Physics

Developed the theory of relativity, the most famous equation E=mc¬≤, and contributed to quantum mechanics. Nobel Prize winner (1921) who fled Nazi Germany as a Jewish refugee.

MJ

Mae C. Jemison (1956-)

United States | Engineering & Medicine

First African American woman to travel to space (1992). Engineer, physician, and NASA astronaut. Conducted experiments on motion sickness and bone cells in microgravity.

NT

Nikola Tesla (1856-1943)

Serbia/United States | Electrical Engineering

Serbian American inventor who developed alternating current (AC) electrical systems, induction motor, and Tesla coil. His work made modern power distribution possible.

DB

Daniel Hale Williams (1856-1931)

United States | Medicine

African American surgeon who performed one of the first successful open-heart surgeries. Founded Provident Hospital, the first non-segregated hospital in the United States.

MG

Mario Molina (1943-2020)

Mexico/United States | Chemistry

Mexican American chemist who discovered how CFCs destroy the ozone layer. Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1995). His work led to the Montreal Protocol, protecting Earth's ozone.

Global Contributions to Physical Science

  • Ancient Greece: Democritus first proposed atoms; Archimedes discovered buoyancy principles
  • Islamic Golden Age: Alhazen (Ibn al-Haytham) pioneered optics and the scientific method
  • Ancient China: Invented the compass (magnetism), gunpowder (chemistry), and discovered natural gas
  • India: Ancient metallurgy, early atomic theory by Kanada, significant contributions to mathematics
  • Mesoamerica: Maya understood astronomy and mathematics; developed sophisticated calendars
  • Africa: Ancient Egyptian chemistry (cosmetics, mummification); Ethiopian steel-making
