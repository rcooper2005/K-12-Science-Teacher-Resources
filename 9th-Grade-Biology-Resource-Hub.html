üìã Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - Biology

These standards align with the Georgia Department of Education Biology curriculum for 9th grade.

SB1: Cell Structure & Function

Students will analyze the nature of relationships between structures and functions in living cells.

  • SB1.a: Explain the role of cell organelles for both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, including the cell membrane, in maintaining homeostasis and cell reproduction.
  • SB1.b: Explain how enzymes function as catalysts.
  • SB1.c: Identify the function of the four major macromolecules (carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, nucleic acids).
  • SB1.d: Explain the impact of water on life processes.
SB2: Cellular Energy

Students will analyze how energy is captured, stored, and used in living systems.

  • SB2.a: Explain the role of enzymes as catalysts that lower activation energy.
  • SB2.b: Explain the role of ATP in storing and releasing energy.
  • SB2.c: Explain the role of photosynthesis and cellular respiration in cycling matter and energy flow.
  • SB2.d: Connect photosynthesis and cellular respiration to ATP production.
SB3: Genetics & Heredity

Students will analyze how biological traits are passed on to successive generations.

  • SB3.a: Explain the role of DNA in storing and transmitting cellular information.
  • SB3.b: Describe DNA structure and function: nucleotides, base pairing, double helix.
  • SB3.c: Explain the process of DNA replication.
  • SB3.d: Describe transcription and translation.
  • SB3.e: Explain the cell cycle, including mitosis and meiosis.
  • SB3.f: Apply Mendelian genetics concepts.
SB4: Evolution & Classification

Students will evaluate the role of natural selection in the development of the theory of evolution.

  • SB4.a: Explain how evidence supports evolution (fossils, comparative anatomy, molecular biology, embryology).
  • SB4.b: Explain how natural selection influences species changes.
  • SB4.c: Explain speciation and reproductive isolation.
  • SB4.d: Use classification systems to identify organisms.
SB5: Ecology & Interdependence

Students will evaluate the interdependence of organisms in ecosystems.

  • SB5.a: Explain energy flow through ecosystems and population responses.
  • SB5.b: Describe matter cycling (carbon, nitrogen, water) through the biosphere.
  • SB5.c: Relate population growth patterns to environmental factors.
  • SB5.d: Evaluate human activities' impact on biodiversity and ecosystems.

üî¨ Cell Structure & Organelles

Cell Theory

Three principles: (1) All organisms are composed of one or more cells, (2) The cell is the basic unit of structure and function, (3) All cells come from pre-existing cells.

Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic

Prokaryotic: No nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles, smaller (1-10 Œºm), includes bacteria and archaea.

Eukaryotic: Has nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, larger (10-100 Œºm), includes plants, animals, fungi, protists.

üö™ Cell Membrane & Transport

Membrane Structure

Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins (fluid mosaic model). Hydrophilic heads face out, hydrophobic tails face in.

Passive Transport (No ATP required)

  • Diffusion: Movement from high to low concentration
  • Osmosis: Diffusion of water across membrane
  • Facilitated Diffusion: Uses transport proteins

Active Transport (Requires ATP)

  • Protein Pumps: Move specific molecules against gradient
  • Endocytosis: Cell engulfs material
  • Exocytosis: Cell expels material

Tonicity

  • Isotonic: Equal concentration, no net water movement
  • Hypertonic: More solute outside, cell shrinks
  • Hypotonic: Less solute outside, cell swells
üîÑ Cell Cycle & Mitosis

Interphase (~90% of cycle)

  • G1: Cell growth, normal functions
  • S: DNA replication
  • G2: Preparation for division

Mitosis Stages (PMAT)

  • Prophase: Chromatin condenses, nuclear envelope breaks down, spindle forms
  • Metaphase: Chromosomes line up at equator
  • Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate to poles
  • Telophase: Nuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense
  • Cytokinesis: Cytoplasm divides

Meiosis

Two divisions producing 4 haploid gametes. Crossing over creates genetic variation.

Photosynthesis

Equation: 6CO‚ÇÇ + 6H‚ÇÇO + light ‚Üí C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ + 6O‚ÇÇ

üß¨ DNA Structure & Replication

DNA Structure

Double helix of nucleotides. Each nucleotide: deoxyribose sugar + phosphate + nitrogenous base.

Base Pairing: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds)

Replication (Semiconservative)

  1. Helicase unwinds double helix
  2. Primase adds RNA primers
  3. DNA Polymerase adds nucleotides (5' to 3')
  4. Ligase joins Okazaki fragments
üî° Protein Synthesis

Central Dogma: DNA ‚Üí RNA ‚Üí Protein

Transcription (Nucleus)

RNA polymerase reads DNA template strand (3' to 5'), builds mRNA (5' to 3'). Uses A-U, T-A, G-C, C-G pairing.

Translation (Ribosome)

  1. mRNA binds to ribosome at start codon (AUG)
  2. tRNA brings amino acids matching each codon
  3. Amino acids join via peptide bonds
  4. Stops at stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA)
üé≤ Mendelian Genetics

Mendel's Laws

  • Dominance: One allele may mask another
  • Segregation: Allele pairs separate during gamete formation
  • Independent Assortment: Genes for different traits sort independently

Key Terms

  • Allele: Different versions of a gene
  • Dominant: Expressed when present (capital letter)
  • Recessive: Only expressed when homozygous (lowercase)
  • Genotype: Genetic makeup (BB, Bb, bb)
  • Phenotype: Physical expression
  • Homozygous: Same alleles (BB or bb)
  • Heterozygous: Different alleles (Bb)

Beyond Mendel

  • Incomplete Dominance: Blended phenotype
  • Codominance: Both alleles expressed
  • Multiple Alleles: More than 2 versions exist
  • Polygenic: Multiple genes control one trait
  • Sex-Linked: Genes on X or Y chromosome
ü¶é Evolution & Natural Selection

Natural Selection Requirements

  1. Variation: Individuals differ
  2. Inheritance: Traits passed to offspring
  3. Selection: Some traits improve survival
  4. Time: Changes accumulate over generations

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil Record: Shows change over time
  • Comparative Anatomy: Homologous/analogous/vestigial structures
  • Molecular Biology: DNA sequence similarities
  • Biogeography: Species distribution patterns
  • Embryology: Similar developmental stages

Speciation

Formation of new species through reproductive isolation (geographic, behavioral, temporal).

üîñ Classification & Taxonomy

Hierarchy

Domain ‚Üí Kingdom ‚Üí Phylum ‚Üí Class ‚Üí Order ‚Üí Family ‚Üí Genus ‚Üí Species

"Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup"

Three Domains

  • Bacteria: Prokaryotic, peptidoglycan walls
  • Archaea: Prokaryotic, extremophiles
  • Eukarya: Eukaryotic (Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia)

Binomial Nomenclature

Genus species - Example: Homo sapiens

üå≥ Ecology & Ecosystems

Levels of Organization

Organism ‚Üí Population ‚Üí Community ‚Üí Ecosystem ‚Üí Biome ‚Üí Biosphere

Energy Flow

  • Producers: Make own food (autotrophs)
  • Consumers: Eat others (heterotrophs)
  • Decomposers: Break down dead matter

10% Rule: Only ~10% of energy transfers between trophic levels.

Biogeochemical Cycles

  • Carbon: Photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, decomposition
  • Nitrogen: Fixation, nitrification, denitrification
  • Water: Evaporation, condensation, precipitation, transpiration

Ecological Relationships

  • Mutualism: Both benefit (+/+)
  • Commensalism: One benefits, other unaffected (+/0)
  • Parasitism: One benefits, other harmed (+/-)
  • Predation: One eats another (+/-)
  • Competition: Both harmed (-/-)

üî¨ Lab 1: Microscope Cell Observation (SB1)

Objectives

  • Use compound light microscope correctly
  • Identify differences between plant and animal cells
  • Observe and identify cell organelles

Materials

Microscopes, slides (onion, Elodea, cheek cells), blank slides, coverslips, methylene blue, iodine, toothpicks, droppers

Procedure

  1. Prepare onion cells with iodine stain
  2. Prepare Elodea leaf wet mount
  3. Prepare cheek cells with methylene blue
  4. Observe each at 40x, 100x, 400x
  5. Draw labeled diagrams

CER Question

How do plant cells differ structurally from animal cells?

Sample: Claim - Plant cells have cell walls, chloroplasts, and large central vacuoles that animal cells lack. Evidence - Onion/Elodea had rigid rectangular shapes with visible walls; Elodea had green chloroplasts; cheek cells had irregular round shapes. Reasoning - Plants need cell walls for structure without skeletons, chloroplasts for photosynthesis, and large vacuoles for turgor pressure.

ü•ö Lab 2: Osmosis with Eggs (SB1)

Objectives

  • Observe effects of osmosis
  • Understand hypertonic, hypotonic, isotonic solutions
  • Collect quantitative data

Materials

3 eggs (shells removed with vinegar), beakers, distilled water, corn syrup, tap water, scale, ruler

Procedure

  1. Measure initial mass/circumference of each egg
  2. Place in labeled cups: A-distilled water, B-corn syrup, C-tap water
  3. After 24 hours, remeasure
  4. Calculate percent change

CER Question

How does solution concentration affect water movement across a membrane?

Water moves from high to low concentration. Distilled water (hypotonic) causes water to enter egg; corn syrup (hypertonic) causes water to exit.

‚öóÔ∏è Lab 3: Enzyme Activity (SB2)

Objectives

  • Investigate factors affecting enzyme activity
  • Understand enzymes as biological catalysts

Materials

Fresh liver/potato (catalase), 3% hydrogen peroxide, test tubes, hot plate, ice bath, pH buffers, ruler, stopwatch

Procedure

  1. Test temperature: samples at 0¬∞C, 25¬∞C, 37¬∞C, 70¬∞C
  2. Add H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ, measure foam height after 30 sec
  3. Test pH: samples in pH 2, 7, 10 solutions
  4. Graph enzyme activity vs. conditions

CER Question

What conditions are optimal for enzyme function?

Enzymes have optimal temperature (~37¬∞C for human enzymes) and pH. Extreme conditions denature enzymes, destroying their active site.

üçì Lab 4: DNA Extraction (SB3)

Objectives

  • Extract DNA from living tissue
  • Understand physical properties of DNA

Materials

Strawberries, zip-lock bag, extraction buffer (10mL soap + 90mL water + ¬º tsp salt), cold 91% isopropyl alcohol, coffee filter, funnel, wooden stick

Procedure

  1. Mash strawberry in sealed bag
  2. Add extraction buffer, mix gently
  3. Filter through coffee filter
  4. Add cold alcohol slowly
  5. Spool white DNA strands with stick

CER Question

Why can we see DNA after extraction?

Soap breaks membranes, salt helps DNA clump, cold alcohol precipitates DNA. Millions of DNA strands together become visible.

üßÖ Lab 5: Mitosis in Onion Root Tips (SB3)

Objectives

  • Identify stages of mitosis in plant cells
  • Calculate mitotic index

Materials

Prepared onion root tip slides, microscopes, data tables

Procedure

  1. Locate meristematic region
  2. Identify and count cells in each stage
  3. Count at least 50 cells
  4. Calculate percentages and mitotic index

CER Question

Which phase do cells spend most time in?

~90% in interphase because DNA replication and cell growth take longer than division.

ü¶ã Lab 6: Natural Selection Simulation (SB4)

Objectives

  • Model how natural selection changes allele frequencies
  • Understand variation and selective pressure

Materials

Colored paper dots/beads (100+ per color), fabric backgrounds, timer, data tables

Procedure

  1. Start with equal numbers of each color "organism"
  2. Scatter on fabric "environment"
  3. "Predators" pick up organisms for 30 seconds
  4. Count survivors, have them "reproduce" (double)
  5. Repeat for 5 generations
  6. Graph population changes

CER Question

How does environment affect trait frequency in a population?

Camouflaged organisms survive and reproduce more, increasing their allele frequency over generations.

üå± Lab 8: Ecosystem in a Bottle (SB5)

Article 1: The Secret Life Inside Your Cells

Every second, trillions of cells work together to keep you alive. These microscopic factories are the basic building blocks of all living things.

Scientists use the cell theory to describe three ideas: all living organisms are made of cells, the cell is the basic unit of life, and all cells come from existing cells.

Inside each cell, organelles perform specific jobs. The nucleus contains DNA with instructions. Mitochondria convert nutrients into energy. In plant cells, chloroplasts capture sunlight for photosynthesis.

The cell membrane controls what enters and exits, described as "selectively permeable."

Comprehension Questions

  1. What are the three main ideas of cell theory?
  2. Why are mitochondria called "powerhouses"?
  3. How does the cell membrane protect the cell?

Article 2: DNA - The Code of Life

If you uncoiled all DNA from one cell, it would be about six feet long‚Äîyet it fits in a microscopic nucleus. DNA contains instructions for building every living thing.

DNA's structure is a twisted ladder called a double helix. The "rungs" are base pairs: adenine with thymine, guanine with cytosine.

In 1953, Watson and Crick described DNA's structure using X-ray images from Rosalind Franklin. DNA replication ensures each new cell gets complete genetic instructions.

Your DNA contains about 20,000 genes. Humans share 99.9% of DNA with each other‚Äîthe 0.1% difference makes each person unique.

Comprehension Questions

  1. What does DNA's structure look like?
  2. What base pairs with adenine?
  3. Why was Rosalind Franklin's work important?

Article 3: Evolution - Change Over Time

In 1831, Charles Darwin sailed on the HMS Beagle. On the Gal√°pagos Islands, he noticed finches with different beak shapes, leading to his theory of natural selection.

Natural selection has four steps: variation (individuals differ), traits are heritable, competition exists, and individuals with better-suited traits survive and reproduce more.

Over generations, helpful traits become common‚Äîthis is evolution. Evidence includes the fossil record and homologous structures (similar bone patterns in whale flippers, bat wings, and human arms).

Comprehension Questions

  1. What did Darwin observe about Gal√°pagos finches?
  2. What are the four requirements for natural selection?
  3. What are homologous structures?

Article 4: Energy Flow in Ecosystems

Energy flows one direction: sun ‚Üí producers ‚Üí consumers ‚Üí decomposers. Producers (autotrophs) capture sunlight through photosynthesis.

Consumers (heterotrophs) must eat others. Primary consumers eat producers; secondary consumers eat primary consumers. Each level is a trophic level.

The 10% rule: only about 10% of energy transfers between levels. The rest is used for life processes or lost as heat‚Äîexplaining why there are fewer predators than prey.

Decomposers break down dead matter, recycling nutrients for producers.

Comprehension Questions

  1. What's the difference between producers and consumers?
  2. Explain the 10% rule.
  3. Why are there fewer predators than prey?

Article 5: Heredity - Passing Traits

Gregor Mendel discovered that traits are controlled by genes. Each organism inherits two versions (alleles) of each gene‚Äîone from each parent.

Dominant alleles mask recessive ones. Recessive alleles are only expressed when homozygous. Punnett squares predict offspring trait probabilities.

Some traits show incomplete dominance (blending), others are controlled by multiple genes (polygenic inheritance).

Comprehension Questions

  1. What's the difference between a gene and an allele?
  2. How do dominant and recessive alleles differ?
  3. What is a Punnett square used for?

Article 6: The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle has two parts: interphase (~90%) and mitosis. During interphase, cells grow, copy DNA, and prepare for division.

Mitosis has four stages (PMAT): prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase. After mitosis, cytokinesis divides the cytoplasm, creating two identical daughter cells.

Checkpoints ensure everything is correct before proceeding. When control fails, cells divide uncontrollably‚Äîcausing cancer.

Comprehension Questions

  1. What happens during interphase?
  2. What are the four stages of mitosis?
  3. How are checkpoints related to cancer?

General and topic-specific safety protocols for biology labs.

‚ö†Ô∏è General Lab Safety Rules

  • Always wear appropriate PPE (safety goggles, gloves, lab coat when required)
  • Know the location of safety equipment (eyewash, fire extinguisher, first aid)
  • Never eat, drink, or apply cosmetics in the lab
  • Tie back long hair and secure loose clothing
  • Report all accidents and spills to the teacher immediately
  • Wash hands thoroughly before leaving the lab
  • Follow all written and verbal instructions carefully
  • Never work alone in the laboratory
üî¨ Microscope Safety & Care
  • Always carry microscope with two hands (arm and base)
  • Start with lowest power objective when focusing
  • Never use coarse adjustment with high power
  • Clean lenses only with lens paper
  • Never touch lenses with fingers
  • Lower stage before removing slides
  • Store with lowest objective in place, cord wrapped
üß™ Chemical/Stain Handling
  • Wear gloves when handling stains (methylene blue, iodine)
  • Iodine and methylene blue will stain skin and clothing
  • Work in well-ventilated areas
  • Never pipette by mouth
  • Dispose of chemicals in designated containers
  • Flush skin immediately if contact occurs
ü¶† Biological Specimen Handling
  • Treat all biological materials as potentially infectious
  • Always wear gloves when handling specimens
  • Never touch face while working with specimens
  • Dispose of biological waste in designated biohazard containers
  • Disinfect work surfaces before and after labs
  • Report any cuts or punctures immediately
üî™ Sharps Safety
  • Always cut away from body
  • Use appropriate cutting tools for the task
  • Never leave sharp objects unattended
  • Dispose of broken glass in designated containers
  • Report any injuries involving sharps immediately
  • Scalpels and razor blades go in sharps containers
üî• Heat Sources
  • Know how to properly light and extinguish Bunsen burners
  • Never leave heat sources unattended
  • Use appropriate tools for handling hot materials
  • Point test tubes away from people when heating
  • Allow materials to cool before handling
  • Keep flammable materials away from heat

üéØ Differentiation Strategies

Strategies for diverse learners including below/above grade level, ELL, and IEP/504.

üìâ Below Grade Level

  • Vocabulary: Pre-teach key terms, use word walls, provide glossaries with visuals
  • Reading: Provide highlighted texts, audio versions, graphic organizers
  • Instruction: Chunk information, use more visuals and manipulatives
  • Assessment: Allow verbal responses, reduce answer choices, provide word banks
  • Support: Pair with peer tutors, provide study guides, allow extended time
  • Labs: Provide step-by-step visual guides, pre-cut materials, extra scaffolding

üìà Above Grade Level

  • Depth: Explore molecular mechanisms, read primary research articles
  • Extension: Independent research projects, science fair, competitions
  • Leadership: Peer tutoring, lab group leaders, presentation opportunities
  • Complexity: Multi-step problems, real-world applications, ethical debates
  • Resources: Advanced reading materials, college-level videos, AP content
  • Labs: Design own experiments, analyze authentic data, write research papers

üåç English Language Learners

  • Vocabulary: Visual dictionaries, cognates, native language resources
  • Visuals: Diagrams, videos, real objects, demonstrations
  • Language: Sentence frames, word banks, simplified instructions
  • Grouping: Strategic pairing, same-language partners when helpful
  • Assessment: Allow drawings, reduce language demands, oral responses
  • Support: Bilingual glossaries, translated materials, extended time

üìã IEP/504 Accommodations

  • Presentation: Large print, audio, preferential seating, reduced visual clutter
  • Response: Verbal answers, scribe, typed responses, alternative formats
  • Timing: Extended time, frequent breaks, flexible deadlines
  • Setting: Small group testing, reduced distractions, movement breaks
  • Organization: Checklists, graphic organizers, color-coded materials
  • Behavior: Clear expectations, positive reinforcement, movement opportunities

Always follow individual student IEP/504 plans as written.

‚ùì Common Misconceptions

Address these common student misconceptions with corrections.

‚ùå "Humans evolved from monkeys"
‚úì Humans and modern apes share a common ancestor that lived millions of years ago. We are cousins, not descendants.
‚ùå "All cells look the same"
‚úì Cells have diverse shapes and structures based on their function. Neurons, muscle cells, and red blood cells look very different.
‚ùå "Genes and DNA are different things"
‚úì Genes ARE sections of DNA. DNA is the molecule; genes are specific segments that code for proteins.
‚ùå "Photosynthesis only happens during the day"
‚úì The light reactions require light, but the Calvin Cycle can occur anytime. Plants also perform cellular respiration 24/7.
‚ùå "Dominant traits are more common"
‚úì Dominant only means expressed when present. Many dominant alleles are rare in populations (e.g., polydactyly).
‚ùå "Evolution is 'just a theory'"
‚úì In science, a theory is a well-supported explanation backed by extensive evidence. Theories are the highest level of scientific explanation.
‚ùå "Plants get food from the soil"
‚úì Plants MAKE their own food (glucose) through photosynthesis using CO‚ÇÇ from air and water. Soil provides minerals, not food.
‚ùå "Organisms can evolve traits they need"
‚úì Evolution has no goal or direction. Random mutations occur; natural selection favors beneficial ones. Organisms cannot "want" to evolve.
‚ùå "Bigger organisms have more cells"
‚úì Cell SIZE is relatively constant across organisms. Bigger organisms have MORE cells, not bigger cells.
‚ùå "All bacteria are harmful"
‚úì Most bacteria are harmless or beneficial. We have more bacterial cells than human cells! Gut bacteria aid digestion.
‚ùå "Respiration and breathing are the same"
‚úì Breathing is the physical movement of air. Cellular respiration is the chemical process of breaking down glucose for energy.
‚ùå "All mutations are harmful"
‚úì Most mutations are neutral. Some are harmful, some beneficial. Beneficial mutations are the basis for evolution.

üåè Cultural Contributions to Biology

  • Traditional Chinese Medicine: Led to discovery of artemisinin for malaria (Tu Youyou's Nobel Prize work)
  • Indigenous Knowledge: Centuries of plant knowledge contributed to modern medicine and agriculture
  • Islamic Golden Age: Ibn al-Nafis described pulmonary circulation; foundations of experimental method
  • African Agriculture: Ancient cultivation techniques; domestication of crops like sorghum and yams
  • Mesoamerican Science: Domesticated corn from teosinte; development of diverse crops
  • Aboriginal Australian Knowledge: 65,000+ years of sustainable land management and plant medicine
