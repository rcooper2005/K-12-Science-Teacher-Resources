College Board Big Ideas

Big Idea 1: Systems & Interactions

Objects and systems have properties such as mass and charge. Systems may have internal structure. Fields existing in space can be used to explain interactions.

Big Idea 2: Fields

A field associates a value of some physical quantity with every point in space. Field models can be used to explain and predict interactions between objects.

Big Idea 3: Force Interactions

The interactions of an object with other objects can be described by forces. Forces are vectors that can be added together.

Big Idea 4: Change & Conservation Laws

Interactions between systems can result in changes in those systems. Changes that occur as a result of interactions are constrained by conservation laws.

Big Idea 5: Waves & Wave Properties

Waves can transfer energy and momentum without the transfer of matter. Wave behavior can be observed in interference and diffraction patterns.

Big Idea 6: Energy (Thermodynamics)

The total energy of an isolated system is conserved. Energy can be transferred between systems through various mechanisms.

Big Idea 7: Quantum Physics

The properties of matter and electromagnetic radiation can be explained by quantum mechanics. Light exhibits wave-particle duality.

Gyroscopes: The Physics of Stability

üìä Lexile: 1250üìö Unit 1: Rotational Motion

From smartphone navigation to spacecraft control, gyroscopes demonstrate conservation of angular momentum. When the wheel spins rapidly, it exhibits remarkable stability. This behavior stems from L = Iœâ. The faster the wheel spins, the more it resists changes to its orientation.

When a force attempts to tilt a spinning gyroscope, it undergoes precession‚Äîa slow rotation perpendicular to both the gravitational torque and spin axis.

Questions: What physical principle explains gyroscopic stability? Why does a spinning gyroscope precess rather than fall?

The Physics of Flight

üìä Lexile: 1280üìö Unit 4: Fluid Mechanics

Bernoulli's principle and Newton's Third Law both contribute to lift. Wings shaped as airfoils cause air to travel faster over the upper surface, creating a pressure differential.

The angle of attack deflects air downward, producing an equal and opposite upward force. The continuity equation (A‚ÇÅv‚ÇÅ = A‚ÇÇv‚ÇÇ) explains why air speeds up over curved surfaces.

Questions: Why is Bernoulli's principle alone insufficient to explain lift? How does angle of attack contribute?

Entropy: The Arrow of Time

üìä Lexile: 1300üìö Unit 5: Thermodynamics

Entropy gives direction to time itself. The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that entropy tends to increase. Boltzmann expressed this: S = k ln(W), where W represents microstates.

The Second Law explains why heat engines cannot be perfectly efficient. The Carnot efficiency (Œ∑ = 1 - Tc/T‚Çï) represents the maximum possible efficiency.

Questions: How does entropy relate to microstates? How does the Second Law limit heat engine efficiency?

Lightning: Nature's Electric Discharge

üìä Lexile: 1240üìö Unit 6: Electrostatics

A lightning bolt releases approximately 1 billion joules of energy. Understanding this requires examining electric fields, charge separation, and dielectric breakdown.

Rising ice crystals collide with falling graupel, creating intense electric fields exceeding 100,000 V/m. At ~3 million V/m, air undergoes dielectric breakdown. The potential difference can reach 100 million volts.

Questions: How does charge separation occur in clouds? What conditions cause dielectric breakdown?

MRI: Seeing Inside with Magnetism

üìä Lexile: 1270üìö Unit 8: Magnetism

Magnetic Resonance Imaging uses nuclear magnetic properties and electromagnetic induction. Hydrogen protons act as tiny magnetic dipoles.

Radiofrequency pulses at the Larmor frequency tip magnetization. As protons relax, detection coils receive signals through electromagnetic induction. Gradient coils enable spatial localization.

Questions: Why are hydrogen nuclei useful for MRI? How do gradient coils enable spatial localization?

Solar Cells: Quantum Physics at Work

üìä Lexile: 1260üìö Unit 10: Modern Physics

The photovoltaic effect converts light to electricity. In semiconductors, electrons occupy discrete energy bands. The band gap determines minimum photon energy needed.

Solar cells use p-n junctions to separate light-generated electron-hole pairs. Multi-junction cells with different band gaps capture more of the solar spectrum, pushing efficiencies above 40%.

Questions: Why can't all photons contribute to solar cell current? How do multi-junction cells achieve higher efficiency?

‚ö†Ô∏è Laboratory Safety

‚ö†Ô∏è General Laboratory Safety

  • Safety goggles required for all laboratory activities
  • Know locations: fire extinguisher, eyewash, first aid, fire blanket
  • No food or drink in the laboratory
  • Report all accidents immediately
  • Never perform unauthorized experiments

‚ö° High Voltage Safety (Circuits, Electrostatics)

  • Never exceed equipment voltage ratings
  • Discharge capacitors before handling - use discharge resistor
  • Van de Graaff: Students with pacemakers must not participate
  • Keep one hand behind back with high voltage (prevents heart path)
  • Ensure all connections secure before powering circuits

üî¶ Laser Safety (Optics)

  • Never look directly into laser beam
  • Never point laser at another person's face
  • Be aware of reflections from mirrors, lenses, shiny surfaces
  • Use Class 1 or Class 2 lasers only
  • Keep beams at table height, below eye level

üíß Pressure & Fluid Safety

  • Inspect containers for cracks before pressurizing
  • Never exceed rated pressure limits
  • Use shatter-resistant containers when possible
  • Clean up water spills immediately

üî• Hot Plate & Calorimetry Safety

  • Use heat-resistant gloves for hot objects
  • Never leave hot plates unattended
  • Keep flammables away from heat sources
  • Allow equipment to cool before storing

‚ò¢Ô∏è Radioactive Source Handling (Modern Physics)

  • Only use exempt quantity sealed sources approved for education
  • Never open sealed sources
  • Minimize time near sources - use tweezers
  • Store in designated, locked cabinet
  • Document all source usage in radiation log

Emergency Procedures

Electrical Shock: Turn off power. Do not touch victim if in contact with current. Call for help. Begin CPR if needed.

Burns: Cool with running water 10+ minutes. Do not apply ice. Seek medical attention for severe burns.

Fire: Alert others, activate alarm, evacuate. Use extinguisher only for small, contained fires.

‚ùå Common Misconceptions

Addressing misconceptions is crucial for deep understanding. Use these as formative assessment probes and discussion starters.

‚ùå Misconception

"Angular velocity and linear velocity are the same thing."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Angular velocity (œâ) measures rotation rate in rad/s, while linear velocity (v) is speed in m/s. They're related by v = œâr - same œâ produces different v at different radii.

‚ùå Misconception

"Heat and temperature are the same thing."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Temperature measures average kinetic energy. Heat is energy transfer due to temperature difference. A large cold object can transfer more heat than a small hot object.

‚ùå Misconception

"Capacitors store charge."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Capacitors store energy in the electric field. Total charge is zero - equal and opposite charges on each plate. "Charge stored" refers to magnitude on one plate.

‚ùå Misconception

"Light slows down permanently when entering glass."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Light travels slower in glass due to interactions with atoms, but returns to c upon exiting. Photon energy unchanged; only speed in medium affected.

‚ùå Misconception

"Electrons orbit like planets orbit the sun."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Electrons exist in probability clouds (orbitals), not defined paths. Energy is quantized - specific energy levels only. Bohr model is useful approximation but not physically accurate.

‚ùå Misconception

"Heavier objects oscillate more slowly on springs."

‚úì Correct Understanding

This IS correct for springs (T = 2œÄ‚àö(m/k)), but NOT for pendulums. Pendulum period depends on length and g, not mass. Students often incorrectly generalize.

‚ùå Misconception

"Current is 'used up' as it flows through a circuit."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Current (charge flow rate) is the same throughout series circuit - charge is conserved. Voltage decreases across resistors. Resistors convert electrical energy to heat, not consume current.

‚ùå Misconception

"Objects float because they're lighter than water."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Objects float when average density is less than fluid density. Steel ships float because hollow shape gives low average density despite steel being denser than water.

‚ùå Misconception

"Magnetic force can do work on charges."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity (F = qv √ó B), so cannot do work. Magnetic force changes direction, not speed. KE remains constant.

‚ùå Misconception

"Brighter light always ejects more electrons."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Only if frequency exceeds threshold. Very bright red light won't eject any electrons below threshold. Brightness affects number only after threshold met.

‚ùå Misconception

"Entropy means disorder, so life violates the second law."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Second law applies to isolated systems. Living organisms are open systems that decrease local entropy by increasing entropy elsewhere through metabolism. Total entropy still increases.

‚ùå Misconception

"Virtual images don't exist - you can't project them."

‚úì Correct Understanding

Virtual images are real - you see yourself in mirrors! They form where light rays appear to diverge from. Can't project because light doesn't actually pass through, but eye/camera can focus on them.
